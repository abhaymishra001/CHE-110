📊 Three Pillars of Sustainability

Sustainable development rests on three interconnected pillars:

  • Economic Sustainability: Ensuring long-term economic growth without depleting natural capital. Examples: Green jobs, circular economy, sustainable agriculture.

  • Environmental Sustainability: Protecting natural resources, reducing pollution, and maintaining ecosystem health. Includes biodiversity conservation and climate action.
Topic 1

Introduction to Environment

🌿 What is Environment?

The word Environment is derived from the French word "Environner" meaning "to surround". Environment refers to everything that surrounds a living organism — both living and non-living components that affect and influence the survival, growth, development, and behaviour of organisms.

A pristine waterfall ecosystem — Nature in its purest form 🌿

The word 'Environment' is derived from the French word "Environer" meaning 'to surround'. Everything that surrounds us — air, water, soil, living organisms, sunlight — constitutes our environment. The study of these interrelationships is called Environmental Studies, a multidisciplinary field that integrates ecology, biology, chemistry, geography, sociology, economics, and law.

In simpler words: Environment = Everything around us! The air we breathe, the water we drink, the soil under our feet, the plants, animals, mountains, rivers — all of it is our environment.

📌 Remember this! Environment includes all external conditions and factors — physical, chemical, and biological — that act upon an organism or community of organisms and ultimately determine their form and survival.

🗂️ Types of Environment

Environment is broadly classified into two major types:

🌱 Natural Environment

All naturally occurring components — air, water, soil, forests, mountains, rivers, oceans, animals, plants. Created by nature, not by humans.

🏙️ Man-made (Built) Environment

All components created by human activities — cities, roads, dams, buildings, factories, farms, parks. Modified from nature by humans.

Sub-types of Natural Environment

  • Physical Environment — Non-living (abiotic) components: air, water, soil, sunlight, temperature, minerals
  • Biological Environment — Living (biotic) components: plants, animals, microorganisms, humans
  • Social Environment — Human society, culture, economics, politics, and relationships

⚠️ What Have We Done? (Threats to Environment)

Human activities have severely damaged our environment. Here are the major issues:

  • Deforestation — Cutting of forests for agriculture, urbanisation
  • Air Pollution — Burning fossil fuels, industrial emissions
  • Water Pollution — Industrial waste, sewage disposal in rivers
  • Land Pollution — Plastic waste, solid waste dumping
  • Climate Change — Rising CO₂ levels causing global warming
  • Biodiversity Loss — Species going extinct at alarming rates
  • Overpopulation — Exponential human population growth straining resources
  • Ozone Layer Depletion — CFCs destroying the protective ozone layer
🚨 Fact: Earth's CO₂ levels have risen from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420 ppm today — the highest in 800,000 years! Global temperatures have already risen ~1.1°C above pre-industrial levels.

Components of Environment

🧩 The Four Major Components

Environment has four major components — often called the four spheres of Earth:

Component Description Examples
Atmosphere Gaseous layer surrounding Earth Air, clouds, ozone layer
Hydrosphere All water bodies on Earth Oceans, rivers, lakes, groundwater
Lithosphere Solid outer layer of Earth Soil, rocks, minerals, mountains
Biosphere Zone where life exists All living organisms + their environment

Components of Environment — Natural, Human-made, and Human 🌍

🔍 Detailed Breakdown

Biotic Components include all living organisms — from microscopic bacteria and fungi (decomposers) to plants (producers) and animals (consumers). These interact through complex food chains and food webs, creating a balanced ecosystem.

Abiotic Components include non-living elements like temperature, humidity, pH, minerals, topography, and climate. These set the stage for life and determine which organisms can survive in a habitat.

Energy Components — Solar energy drives nearly all ecosystems on Earth. It fuels photosynthesis in producers and flows through the food chain, losing ~90% at each trophic level (10% Rule of Ecology).

🔑 Key Point: The Biosphere is the thin layer of Earth where all life exists. It overlaps with parts of the atmosphere, hydrosphere, and lithosphere. It extends roughly 10 km below sea level to 10 km above sea level.
Topic 3

Spheres of Earth

🌐 Overview of Earth's Spheres

Earth's environment is divided into four major "spheres." Think of them as four giant systems that interact with each other to make life possible on Earth:

🗻 Lithosphere

Lithos = Stone

The solid, rocky outer shell of Earth. It includes the crust and upper mantle.

  • Made up of hard solid rocks, soil, and loose sediments
  • Extends to about 100 km depth
  • Contains minerals like copper, iron, coal, ruby, diamond
  • Divided into tectonic plates that move very slowly
  • Provides habitat, nutrients, support for all land organisms

💧 Hydrosphere

Hydros = Water

All the water found on, under, and above Earth's surface.

  • Covers ~71% of Earth's surface
  • Includes oceans, rivers, lakes, groundwater, glaciers
  • 97% saltwater; only 3% freshwater
  • Regulates climate via the water cycle
  • Essential for all life processes

💨 Atmosphere

Atmos = Vapour/Air

The gaseous envelope surrounding Earth, held by gravity.

  • Composition: N₂ (78%), O₂ (21%), Ar (0.9%), CO₂ (0.04%)
  • Layers: Troposphere, Stratosphere, Mesosphere, Thermosphere, Exosphere
  • Ozone layer in stratosphere protects from UV radiation
  • Regulates temperature via greenhouse effect
  • Essential for breathing and photosynthesis

🌱 Biosphere

Bios = Life

The zone of Earth where living organisms exist and interact.

  • Extends from deep ocean (-10 km) to high mountains (+10 km)
  • Includes all ecosystems — forest, ocean, desert, tundra
  • Organisms interact through food webs and nutrient cycles
  • Maintained by energy from Sun and nutrients from Earth
  • Currently under serious threat from human activities

🔄 Atmosphere Layers — Quick Reference

Layer Height Key Feature
Troposphere 0–12 km Weather occurs here; temperature decreases with altitude
Stratosphere 12–50 km Contains ozone layer; temperature increases with altitude
Mesosphere 50–80 km Coldest layer; meteors burn up here
Thermosphere 80–700 km Very hot; auroras occur here
Exosphere >700 km Outermost layer; merges with outer space

🔗 Interactions Between Spheres

The spheres don't work in isolation — they constantly interact:

  • Lithosphere + Hydrosphere → Soil formation, weathering of rocks by water
  • Atmosphere + Hydrosphere → Water cycle (evaporation, condensation, precipitation)
  • Biosphere + Lithosphere → Plants take nutrients from soil; organisms decompose and add organic matter to soil
  • Biosphere + Atmosphere → Photosynthesis (plants absorb CO₂, release O₂); Respiration (animals absorb O₂, release CO₂)
  • Biosphere + Hydrosphere → Aquatic life; water is essential for all biological processes

Multidisciplinary Nature of Environmental Studies

🔬 Why is Environmental Studies Multidisciplinary?

Multidisciplinary means a subject that draws knowledge from many different disciplines or fields of study. Environmental Studies is multidisciplinary because environmental problems are complex and cannot be solved by a single field alone.

Modern intensive agriculture — A major pressure on land and water resources 🚜

India has only 2.4% of the world's land area but supports ~18% of the world's population. This puts enormous pressure on our natural resources — water, soil, and forests. Per capita availability of land in India has decreased from 0.89 hectares (1951) to about 0.27 hectares today!

Environmental issues involve physical, chemical, biological, social, economic, and political dimensions — so experts from many fields must work together!

That's the power of multidisciplinary thinking!

🎯 Scope of Environmental Studies

Environmental Studies covers a very wide range of topics:

  • Natural Resources — Study of land, water, forest, mineral, energy resources
  • Ecosystems — Structure, function, food chains, energy flow, ecological succession
  • Biodiversity — Genetic, species, ecosystem diversity; conservation strategies

  • Social Issues — Population growth, environmental movements, ethics

Topic 6 & 7

Concept of Sustainability & Sustainable Development

♻️ What is Sustainability?

Sustainability is the ability to meet current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. It means living within the means of our planet — using resources wisely so they don't run out.

Think of it this way: You have a bank account (Earth's resources). If you spend more than you earn, the account will eventually go empty. Sustainability means spending only what the Earth can replenish.

"The greatest threat to our planet is the belief that someone else will save it." — Robert Swan, Polar Explorer

The Three Pillars of Sustainability

Sustainability rests on three interconnected pillars — also called the "Triple Bottom Line":

💰

Economic

Economic growth that is profitable, creates jobs, and is maintained long-term without depleting resources

👥

Social

Social equity, quality of life, health, education, and well-being for all people, now and in the future

🌿

Environmental

Protection of natural resources, biodiversity, and ecological processes that support all life on Earth

🔑 The Intersection: True sustainability exists where all three pillars overlap. Economic growth without environmental protection is not sustainable. Social welfare without economic viability is not sustainable. Environmental protection that ignores human needs is also not sustainable. All three must be balanced!

🌱 Sustainable Development

Sustainable Development is "development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs." — Brundtland Report (Our Common Future, 1987), WCED

Sustainable Development is the practical application of sustainability — it means designing economic and social development in a way that protects the environment.

From Unsustainable → Sustainable Model

Current unsustainable practices that need to change:

Unsustainable Practice Sustainable Alternative
Burning coal and fossil fuels Solar, wind, and other renewable energy
Linear economy (take → make → waste) Circular economy (reduce → reuse → recycle)
Deforestation Afforestation + sustainable forest management
Chemical-intensive agriculture Organic, precision, and sustainable agriculture
Single-use plastic Biodegradable / reusable alternatives
Urban sprawl and car dependence Compact cities, public transport, walkability
Overexploitation of fish stocks Sustainable fisheries management
Landfill waste disposal Composting, recycling, waste-to-energy

📊 Carrying Capacity

Carrying Capacity is the maximum population size (of humans, animals, or any species) that an environment can support sustainably — without degrading the natural resources that the population depends on.

Formula concept: When a population exceeds the environment's carrying capacity, resources get depleted, waste accumulates, and eventually the population crashes. This is the overshoot and collapse model.

🎭 Was Thanos Right? The Marvel villain Thanos wanted to wipe out half of all life to prevent overpopulation from depleting resources. While his method was genocidal and wrong, the underlying concern about carrying capacity is a real scientific concept. Earth does have limits. The solution, however, is sustainable development — not destruction, but smarter use of resources!

Key Points About Carrying Capacity:

  • Carrying capacity is not fixed — it can increase with technology (e.g., Green Revolution increased food carrying capacity)
  • It can also decrease — when resources are destroyed (e.g., deforestation reduces land's carrying capacity)
  • Factors affecting carrying capacity: food, water, shelter, space, disease, predation
  • Human carrying capacity is estimated between 8–16 billion (Earth currently has ~8.1 billion people)
  • The Earth's carrying capacity is impacted by our consumption patterns — a planet of vegetarians can support more people than a planet of meat-eaters

Measures for Sustainable Development

  • Education & Awareness — Public education on environmental issues
  • Clean Technology — Renewable energy, electric vehicles, green buildings
  • Reduce Consumption — Lower resource use per person
  • Better Governance — Stronger environmental laws and enforcement
  • International Cooperation — Paris Agreement, Kyoto Protocol
  • Circular Economy — Minimise waste, maximise resource reuse
  • Population Stabilisation — Education, women's empowerment, family planning
  • Biodiversity Conservation — Protect ecosystems that provide services

Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

🎯 What are the SDGs?

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) are 17 global goals adopted by all United Nations member states in September 2015, as part of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. They are a universal call to action to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure peace and prosperity by 2030.
📌 Key Facts: 193 countries adopted the SDGs • 17 Goals • 169 Targets • 232 Indicators • Deadline: 2030 • They replaced the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) which ran from 2000–2015

📊 SDG Indicators & Country Performance

SDG Indicators are measurable ways to track progress toward the SDGs. For each of the 17 goals, there are specific numerical indicators.

Examples of SDG Indicators:

  • SDG 1 — Proportion of population below international poverty line ($2.15/day)
  • SDG 3 — Under-5 mortality rate per 1,000 live births
  • SDG 6 — Proportion of population using safely managed drinking water
  • SDG 7 — Renewable energy share in total final energy consumption
  • SDG 13 — CO₂ emissions per capita
📈 Country Rankings: The SDG Index (at dashboards.sdgindex.org) ranks countries on SDG performance. Nordic countries (Finland, Sweden, Denmark) consistently top the rankings. India ranks in the middle (around 112–120 out of 166 countries). Countries can track their progress on individual indicators on the official UN SDG dashboard.

India's SDG Progress:

  • India scores well on SDG 7 (clean energy) with rapid solar expansion
  • Challenges remain in SDG 2 (nutrition), SDG 3 (health), SDG 5 (gender equality)
  • India launched the National Indicator Framework (NIF) with 286 indicators
  • NITI Aayog publishes annual SDG India Index to rank all states

⚠️ Problems of Sustainable Development

Despite the SDGs and sustainability initiatives, several major challenges remain:

Problem Explanation
Poverty & Inequality Over 700 million people live in extreme poverty; inequality between rich and poor countries is vast
Population Growth Rapid population growth (especially in developing countries) puts enormous pressure on resources
Overconsumption High consumption in developed countries; a small fraction of world population uses most of the resources
Climate Change Impacts most vulnerable communities; temperature rise, sea level rise, extreme weather events
Loss of Biodiversity Species extinction rate 1000x above natural background rate; ecosystems being destroyed
Water Scarcity 2 billion people lack access to safe water; groundwater depletion is widespread
Political Will Short-term political cycles don't align with long-term sustainability goals
Financing Gap Estimated $5–7 trillion/year needed for SDGs; current investment far less
Technology Divide Developing nations lack access to clean technologies
Conflict & Instability Wars and political instability reverse development progress in affected regions

Unit Summary

Unit 1 — Complete Summary

📌 Summary of All Topics

  • Environment encompasses all living and non-living elements surrounding organisms. It can be natural or man-made, and is divided into physical, biological, and social sub-types.
  • Spheres of Earth: The four spheres — Lithosphere (solid), Hydrosphere (water), Atmosphere (gas), and Biosphere (life) — interact to sustain life. The atmosphere has five layers with the ozone layer in the stratosphere.
  • Multidisciplinary Nature: Environmental Studies draws from biology, chemistry, physics, geology, geography, economics, sociology, political science, engineering, and ethics. No single field can address all environmental issues alone.
  • Scope & Importance: Environmental Studies covers natural resources, ecosystems, biodiversity, pollution, climate change, disaster management, and policy. It is important for awareness, scientific understanding, professional practice, and social responsibility.
  • Sustainability means living within Earth's limits. The three pillars — economic, social, and environmental — must all be in balance. Sustainability is not just about the environment; it integrates human well-being and economic viability.
  • Sustainable Development (Brundtland definition, 1987): Meeting present needs without compromising future generations. It involves shifting from linear to circular economies, adopting clean technology, and changing consumption patterns.
  • Carrying Capacity: The maximum population an environment can support without degradation. It is dynamic and affected by technology, resource management, and consumption patterns.
  • SDGs: 17 goals adopted by 193 UN member nations in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda. They address poverty, health, education, gender equality, clean energy, climate action, biodiversity, and global partnerships, monitored through 232 indicators.
  • Problems of Sustainable Development: Major challenges include poverty, inequality, climate change, biodiversity loss, water scarcity, overconsumption, political barriers, and financing gaps.
