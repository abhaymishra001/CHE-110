Topic 1

Introduction to Natural Resources

🌍 What are Resources?

A resource is a source or supply from which a benefit is produced and that has some utility. Examples: Land, Minerals, Soil, Water, Energy, Food. Resources may be Natural (from nature), Man-made (created by humans), or Human (human skills, knowledge, labour).

Natural resources are materials given to us by nature which can be transformed to become more valuable and useful. They are the backbone of all economies and ecosystems.

♻️ Renewable vs Non-Renewable Resources

🌱 Renewable Resources

Resources which are renewed or replenished fast, or have an unlimited source. They can be regenerated by natural processes within a human timescale.

  • Solar energy
  • Wind energy
  • Water (via water cycle)
  • Biomass / forests (if managed sustainably)
  • Air, tidal, geothermal energy

⛽ Non-Renewable Resources

Resources which cannot be renewed or replenished within a human timescale. Once used, they are gone forever or take millions of years to form.

  • Fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas)
  • Nuclear fuels (Uranium, Thorium)
  • Minerals and ores
  • Gemstones
📌 Key Distinction: Renewable ≠ unlimited. Forests are renewable but can be destroyed permanently if over-exploited. Water is renewable via the water cycle but can become locally scarce. Sustainable management is essential for all renewable resources.

⭐ Importance of Natural Resources

  • Provide raw materials for food, shelter, clothing, and industry
  • Support all ecosystems and biodiversity
  • Regulate climate and maintain environmental balance
  • Source of energy for all human activities
  • Basis of all national economies
  • Support cultural, recreational, and aesthetic needs
Topic 2

Land Resources

🗻 What is Land Resource?

Land resource = "The solid portion of Earth's surface." The science dealing with land is called Pedology. Land is a renewable but limited resource — it can be restored but its total area is fixed.

Why is land valuable?

  • Agricultural Base — Soil provides nutrients for food production
  • Natural Resources — Land holds minerals, fossil fuels, groundwater
  • Urban Development — Infrastructure, housing, industries
  • Ecosystem Services — Habitats, carbon storage, water filtration

🌱 Soil Formation

Soil is formed by weathering of rocks over thousands of years. Three types of weathering break down parent rock:

  • Physical weathering — Temperature changes, wind, water pressure crack rocks
  • Chemical weathering — Acids, oxidation, hydration dissolve minerals
  • Biological weathering — Roots, microorganisms break rock chemically and mechanically

Factors affecting soil formation:

  • Parent material — The original rock type determines mineral content
  • Living organisms — Add organic matter, mix soil layers
  • Climate — Temperature and rainfall drive weathering rates
  • Topography — Slope affects erosion and water retention
  • Time — Soil formation takes hundreds to thousands of years

Functions of Soil:

  • Facilitates nutrient cycles (N, P, K cycles)
  • Food and biomass production
  • Stores and regulates water supply
  • Regulates emission of trace gases (CH₄, N₂O)
  • Filters and purifies groundwater
  • Provides biological habitat for microorganisms
  • Platform for buildings and infrastructure

💨 Land Degradation

The process by which the fertility and quality of land is reduced due to natural or human activities. Also called "creeping death of land."

🌊 Soil Erosion

Soil erosion is the removal of the top layer of soil from its resting place by physical agencies like wind and water. Also defined as "the detachment and transport of the fertile layer of soil by water or air." It is known as the creeping death of land.

Causes of Soil Erosion:

  • Large-scale deforestation
  • Floods (high-velocity water runoff)
  • Overgrazing (leaves soil bare and exposed)
  • Large violent winds
  • Improper agricultural techniques (no contour farming)

Methods to Control Soil Erosion:

  • Conservation tillage / No-till farming — Minimal soil disturbance; keeps crop residue on soil
  • Contour farming — Plowing along land contours to reduce runoff
  • Terracing — Creating flat steps on hillsides to reduce slope angle
  • Strip cropping — Alternating strips of crops with grass to trap runoff
  • Alley cropping (Agroforestry) — Rows of trees between crop rows; diversifies income + protects soil
  • Windbreaks / Shelterbelts — Rows of trees/shrubs planted to reduce wind velocity
  • Stubble mulching — Leaving crop residue on soil surface as natural mulch (used in dryland and semi-arid areas)
  • Afforestation — Planting trees in deforested or barren areas; roots prevent erosion
  • Control overgrazing — Rotational grazing, setting grazing limits based on carrying capacity
  • Check dams — Temporary structures across streams to slow water flow and reduce soil wash

🏜️ Desertification

Desertification is the process by which the biological productivity of drylands is reduced due to natural or man-made activities. OR: The process by which fertile land becomes desert, typically due to drought, deforestation, or inappropriate agriculture.

Causes:

🌡️ Natural Factors

  • Low rainfall
  • Excessive evaporation
  • High temperature
  • Soil salinity

🏭 Human Factors

  • Deforestation
  • Overgrazing
  • Agricultural expansion
  • Climate change
  • Soil erosion
  • Water mismanagement
  • Mining activities

Control Measures:

  • Large-scale plantation programs
  • Sustainable agricultural practices
  • Development of pasture land; control overgrazing
  • Rainwater harvesting and water catchment development

⛏️ Mining & Its Effects

Mining is the extraction of valuable minerals or other geological materials from the Earth. Two main types: Opencast (surface) mining and Underground mining.

Effects of Mining on Land:

  • Destruction of vegetation and topsoil
  • Land subsidence and instability
  • Soil and water contamination from mine drainage
  • Habitat destruction and biodiversity loss
  • Air pollution from dust and blasting
  • Displacement of local communities

Topic 3

Water Resources

💧 Importance of Water

Water is the most essential substance for life. It covers ~71% of Earth's surface, but only ~3% is freshwater, and less than 1% is easily accessible as surface water.

Special Properties of Water:

  • Universal solvent — Dissolves more substances than any other liquid; essential for biochemical reactions
  • High boiling/melting points — Unusual for a molecule its size; critical for climate regulation
  • Expands when frozen — Ice floats on water (anomalous expansion); prevents lakes from freezing solid
  • Essential for life — Every living cell requires water for metabolic processes

Uses of Water:

  • Basic component of every living cell
  • Agriculture (irrigation) — uses ~70% of all freshwater
  • Industrial cooling, heating, processing — ~20%
  • Domestic use (drinking, cooking, sanitation) — ~10%
  • Hydroelectric power generation
  • Inland waterways transport
  • Source of common salt; habitat for aquatic life

🔄 Water Cycle & Freshwater Sources

The water cycle (hydrological cycle) continuously moves water through evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and runoff, maintaining Earth's water balance.

Sources of Freshwater:

  • Surface water — Rainfall, snow, rivers, lakes, ponds
  • Groundwater — Water stored underground in aquifers (confined and unconfined)
📘 Aquifer: An aquifer is a body of porous rock or sediment saturated with groundwater. Groundwater enters an aquifer as precipitation seeps through soil. It resurfaces through springs and wells.
Confined aquifer = trapped between impermeable rock layers (artesian). Unconfined aquifer = water table aquifer open to atmosphere above.
📊 Global Freshwater Distribution: Top countries by renewable freshwater: Brazil, Russia, Canada, Indonesia, China, USA, India.
Top consumers (total volume): India > China > USA > Indonesia > Pakistan.
Top per-capita consumers: Turkmenistan, Chile, Guyana.

Major Water Conflicts (Important for Exam):

  • Nile conflict (Egypt – Ethiopia – Sudan)
  • Aral Sea conflict (Central Asia)
  • Indus Water Treaty (India–Pakistan): Jhelum, Chenab → Pakistan; Sutlej, Ravi, Beas → India

  • Sutlej–Yamuna Link (SYL) canal dispute (Punjab–Haryana)

🛠️ Remedial Measures for Water Resources

Water Conservation:

  • Decreasing run-off losses
  • Reducing evaporation losses (mulching, cover crops)
  • Storing water in soil (infiltration enhancement)
  • Reducing irrigation losses (drip, sprinkler systems)
  • Re-use and recycling of water (greywater systems)
  • Block pricing to discourage wastage

Rainwater Harvesting:

🌧️ Rainwater Harvesting collects and stores rainwater for later use. Objectives: Meet domestic/industrial/agricultural demand · Recharge groundwater · Minimize pollution · Reduce flooding and runoff. Two methods: (1) Surface runoff harvesting — structures like check dams, tanks, percolation pits; (2) Rooftop rainwater harvesting — direct collection from rooftops.

Watershed Management:

A watershed is an area of land that drains to a common location (small stream to ocean). Watershed management integrates land and water conservation for maximum productivity.

  • Ensures ecological balance
  • Minimizes drought, landslide, and flood risks
  • Proper use of marginal/waste lands
  • Enables domestic water supply, irrigation, hydropower generation

Dams — Benefits & Problems:

✅ Benefits

  • Electricity generation
  • Employment
  • Irrigation water supply
  • Drinking water supply
  • Flood control
  • Reduce famine

❌ Problems

  • Displacement of tribal people
  • Loss of forest
  • Waterlogging near reservoir
  • Microclimatic changes
  • Flash floods
  • Malaria outbreak (vector breeding)
Topic 4

Forest Resources

🌳 What are Forest Resources?

A forest is a biotic community with predominance of trees — an important renewable natural resource. The word "forest" comes from Latin "Fores" meaning "outside." Forests are complex, changing environments made of living (trees, shrubs, wildlife) and non-living components (water, nutrients, rocks, sunlight, air).
📊 Forest Facts: Forests cover ~30% of Earth's total land area (~9.4% of surface). They account for 75% of gross primary productivity of Earth's biosphere and contain 80% of Earth's plant biomass. India's forest + tree cover = 807,276 km² = 24.56% of geographical area (2019 ISFR).

🌿 Benefits / Functions of Forests

FunctionDescriptionExamples
ProtectiveProtection against natural hazards and pollutionPrevents soil erosion, droughts, floods, noise, radiation

AccessoryAesthetic, habitat, and recreational valueWildlife habitat, tourism, biodiversity conservation

Commercial Importance:

  • Timber — Furniture, boats, bridges, construction
  • Fuel wood — Cooking and heating energy for rural populations
  • Raw materials for industries — Paper/pulp, sports goods, furniture, matchboxes
  • Oxygen production — Forest regulates O₂/CO₂ levels; mitigates climate change

🪓 Deforestation — Causes, Effects & Control

Deforestation = The permanent destruction of forests. Forests have been exploited since early human history to meet human demands.

🔥 Causes

  • Fuel requirement (firewood)
  • Raw material for industries
  • Development projects (dams, highways)
  • Expansion of cities
  • Growing food needs (agriculture expansion)
  • Overgrazing
  • Shifting cultivation (Jhum farming)
  • Forest fires
  • Mining activities
  • River valley projects

💥 Effects

  • Threatens wildlife existence
  • Biodiversity and genetic diversity loss
  • Disrupts hydrological cycle
  • Soil erosion and landslides
  • Air pollution and global warming
  • Loss of carbon sinks
  • Reduced rainfall
  • Desertification

Control/Conservation Measures:

  • Afforestation and reforestation programs (e.g. Green India Mission)
  • Sustainable forest management and certified timber
  • Community forest management / Joint Forest Management (JFM)
  • Strict enforcement of forest protection laws
  • Promoting alternative fuel sources to reduce firewood dependence
  • Eco-tourism to generate income from standing forests
Topic 5

Energy Resources

⚡ What is Energy?

Energy = "Capacity to do work." SI Unit = Joule (J). Energy can be converted from one form to another but cannot be created or destroyed (1st Law of Thermodynamics). The ultimate source of most energy on Earth is the Sun.
📊 Global Energy Inequality: Developed countries like USA and Canada constitute ~5% of world population but consume one-fourth of global energy (~300 GJ per person/year = 60 barrels of oil). In contrast, people in poor countries consume less than 1 GJ/year.

Energy Crisis:

A situation where demand for energy exceeds supply, or when there are significant disruptions in energy production or distribution.

  • Over-reliance on non-renewable energy resources
  • Overpopulation (increasing demand)
  • Poor infrastructure/distribution systems
  • Wastage and lack of energy efficiency
  • Major accidents and natural calamities
  • Wars and attacks (e.g. Europe's energy crisis after Russia-Ukraine war)

🌞 Renewable Energy Sources

☀️

Solar Energy

1 hour of solar energy = Earth's 1 year consumption. Uses: heating, solar cooker, solar desalination, PV cells. India target: 100 GW by 2022.

🌬️

Wind Energy

India = 4th largest wind energy producer (after China, US, Germany). Installed capacity ~32 GW. Wind potential: 302 GW at 100m height.

💦

Hydroelectric

Converts potential energy of stored water into electricity via turbines. Low cost, non-polluting, reliable. Disadvantages: high setup cost, affects fish population.

🌿

Biomass Energy

Wood is largest biomass source. Biofuels: biodiesel, biogas, bio-ethanol. India potential: ~18 GW. 32% of India's primary energy = biomass. States: Maharashtra, UP, Karnataka.

🌊

Tidal Energy

Converts tidal force into electricity. More predictable than wind/solar. Disadvantages: EM emissions affect aquatic life; high construction cost.

🌋

Geothermal Energy

Energy from hot rocks inside Earth. Can use natural geysers or drilled holes. Reliable, low operating cost. Possible H₂S/CO₂ emissions during extraction.

⚛️ Non-Renewable Energy Sources

⚛️

Nuclear Energy

Fission: splitting large nucleus (U-235). Fusion: joining small nuclei. Elements: Uranium, Thorium, Plutonium. Nuclear fuel cost = only 20% of energy generated. Continuous production (90% annual time). Risk: accidents, nuclear waste management.

⛽

Fossil Fuels

Coal, oil, natural gas. Current backbone of global energy. Formed over millions of years. Cause air pollution and climate change. Being phased out under Paris Agreement.

⚠️ Limitations of Alternative Energy Sources:
• High installation cost and maintenance requirements
• Raw materials for manufacturing devices not easily available (e.g. Silicon for PV cells)
• Weather dependency (solar/wind not always available)
• Geographical limitations (tidal/geothermal not everywhere viable)
🇮🇳 India's Energy Policy: IEPR target: At least 10% of installed capacity from renewable sources by end of 11th Plan. Solar installed capacity reached 33.73 GW (Dec 2019). India ranked 4th in wind power capacity with ~32 GW installed.

🌿 Role of Individual in Conservation of Natural Resources

  • Reduce, Reuse, Recycle (3R principle)
  • Use energy-efficient appliances (LED bulbs, BEE-rated devices)
  • Switch off lights/fans when not in use; unplug chargers
  • Use public transport, carpool, or cycle
  • Conserve water — fix leaks, rainwater harvesting at home
  • Plant trees and support green initiatives
  • Avoid single-use plastics; use cloth bags
  • Support renewable energy (solar panels at home)
  • Buy local products to reduce transportation energy
  • Spread awareness and educate others
Topic 6

Ecosystem — Structure & Function

🔬 What is an Ecosystem?

Ecosystem = A self-regulating group of biotic communities of species interacting with one another and with their non-living environment, exchanging energy and matter. The term was first used in 1935 by British ecologist Arthur Tansley.
📘 Ecology: The study of organisms in their natural habitat interacting with their surroundings. Term coined by German biologist Ernst Haeckel in 1866. From Greek: Oikos (home) + Logos (study).

🏗️ Structure of an Ecosystem

Every ecosystem has two main components:

🌡️ Abiotic Components (Non-living)

  • Inorganic substances (C, H, O, N, P, S)
  • Organic compounds (proteins, lipids, carbohydrates)
  • Physical factors: temperature, humidity, rainfall, light
  • Soil, water, air

🌿 Biotic Components (Living)

  • Producers (Autotrophs) — Green plants, algae, some bacteria (photosynthesise)
  • Primary consumers — Herbivores (rabbits, insects, deer)
  • Secondary consumers — Carnivores eating herbivores (frog, small fish)
  • Tertiary consumers — Top carnivores (snake, tiger, eagle)
  • Decomposers — Bacteria and fungi breaking down dead organic matter

Food Chain, Food Web & Trophic Levels

🔗 Food Chain

The transfer of food energy from the source (plants) through a series of organisms by repeated eating and being eaten. Each organism is eaten by the next, forming a "chain."

Example — Grazing Food Chain:

🌿 Grass
(Producer)
→
🐇 Rabbit
(1° Consumer)
→
🐍 Snake
(2° Consumer)
→
🦅 Eagle
(3° Consumer)

Types of Food Chains:

  • Grazing food chain — Common chain starting with plant or plant products (grass → grasshopper → frog → snake → eagle)
  • Detritus food chain — Starts with dead organic matter (dead leaves → earthworm → robin → hawk). Derived from organic wastes and dead matter from grazing chains.

🕸️ Food Web

The interlocking pattern formed by several food chains that are linked together is called a food web. A food web represents real feeding relationships more accurately than a single food chain because most organisms eat multiple species.
🌐 Why food webs matter: Food webs show the complexity of energy flow in an ecosystem. If one species is removed, the web helps predict cascading effects on other species. This is called a trophic cascade.

📊 Trophic Levels

Each step in a food chain represents a trophic level. Energy passes from one trophic level to the next:

🦁 Tertiary Consumers — Top Carnivores (T4)
🐍 Secondary Consumers — Carnivores (T3)
🐸 Primary Consumers — Herbivores (T2)
🌿 Producers — Green Plants (T1)
Base of pyramid = most organisms & energy | Apex = least organisms & energy

⚡ Energy Flow in Ecosystems

1st Law of Thermodynamics: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.
2nd Law of Thermodynamics: No energy transformation is 100% efficient. Energy is always lost as heat at each step.
10% Rule (Lindemann's Law): Only 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. 90% is lost as heat, movement, respiration. So: If producers have 10,000 kcal → herbivores get 1,000 kcal → carnivores get 100 kcal → top carnivores get 10 kcal.
Topic 8

Ecological Pyramids

📐 What are Ecological Pyramids?

An ecological pyramid is a graphical representation where producers occupy the base and top consumers occupy the apex. They illustrate the feeding relationships and energy/biomass/numbers at each trophic level.
TypeWhat it showsShapeKey Point
Pyramid of NumberNumber of individuals at each trophic levelUsually upright (grassland/pond); inverted (tree ecosystem)Simplest pyramid; can be misleading (one tree supports many insects)
Pyramid of BiomassTotal dry organic matter at each trophic level (g/m²)Usually upright; inverted in aquatic (phytoplankton)More accurate than number pyramid. Typical values: Producers 2000 g/m² → Primary consumers 675 g/m² → Secondary 150 → Tertiary 75 g/m²
Pyramid of EnergyAmount of energy at each trophic level (kcal/m²/yr)Always upright — NEVER invertedMost accurate representation. Always upright because energy always lost at each level (10% rule)
🔑 Key Exam Point: Pyramid of energy is always upright (never inverted) because energy is always lost as it moves up trophic levels. Pyramids of number and biomass can be inverted in certain ecosystems.
Topic 9

Ecological Succession

🔄 What is Ecological Succession?

Ecological succession is the gradual and predictable process by which ecosystems change and develop over time. It is a series of temporary communities (seral stages) leading up to a stable final climax community. All habitats are in a constant state of ecological succession.

Key Terms:

  • Seral stage / Successional stage — Each temporary community during succession
  • Pioneer community — The first organisms to colonize bare substrate (e.g. lichens on rocks)
  • Climax community — The final stable community that succession leads to (e.g. oak forest)
  • Sere — The entire sequence from pioneer to climax

🌱 Types of Succession

🪨 Primary Succession

Occurs on an entirely new habitat that has never been colonized before. Starts from scratch — no soil, no organic matter.

  • Newly formed volcanic island
  • Freshly quarried rock face
  • Sand dunes
  • Retreating glacier area

Very slow — takes hundreds to thousands of years

🌿 Secondary Succession

Occurs on a previously colonized but disturbed or damaged habitat. Soil and some organisms are still present.

  • Land after felling trees in a forest
  • Land after forest fire
  • Abandoned agricultural fields
  • Area after flood damage

Faster than primary — soil is already present

💧 Aquatic Ecosystems

Pond Ecosystem:

A small freshwater ecosystem with stagnant water. Can be seasonal. Notable features: Small size, stagnant water, highly exposed to human activities.

Lake Ecosystem — Zonation (Stratification):

  • Epilimnion — Warm, lighter, circulating surface layer (sunlight available)
  • Hypolimnion — Cold, viscous, non-circulating bottom layer (dark, deoxygenated)

Types of Lakes:

  • Oligotrophic — Low nutrient content, clear water, deep, high O₂
  • Eutrophic — High nutrient content, algal blooms, low O₂ at bottom
  • Dystrophic — Low pH, brown-coloured water, peat-rich
  • Endemic lakes, Desert salt lakes, Volcanic lakes, Meromictic (permanently stratified), Artificial lakes

Stream Stages:

  • Mountain highland (Young River) — Fast-flowing, turbulent, cold, high O₂
  • Middle-aged River — Slower, wider, more nutrients
  • Old River — Slow, wide, meandering, warm, nutrient-rich

Ocean Zones (from photic to aphotic):

  • Coastal zone — Shallow, most productive, rich in nutrients
  • Euphotic zone — Abundant sunlight, high photosynthetic activity (~200 m depth)
  • Bathyal zone — Dim light, few photosynthesisers (200–4,000 m)
  • Abyssal zone — Complete darkness, extreme pressure, chemosynthetic organisms (>4,000 m)

Aquatic Organisms by Mode of Life:

  • Planktons — Drift with water; algae, rotifers (phytoplankton + zooplankton)
  • Nektons — Active swimmers; fishes
  • Neustons — Live on water surface; water fleas
  • Benthos — Bottom-dwelling; snails, crabs
  • Periphytons — Attached to submerged surfaces; crustaceans

  • Ecology: Ernst Haeckel, 1866
  • Producers = autotrophs
  • Decomposers = bacteria + fungi
  • Abiotic + Biotic = ecosystem

⚡ 10% Energy Rule

  • Only 10% passes to next level
  • 90% lost as heat
  • Pyramid of energy always upright
  • Lindemann's Rule
  • 1000 kcal plants → 100 kcal herbivore

🔄 Succession Types

  • Primary = bare new habitat
  • Secondary = disturbed existing habitat
  • Hydrosere = starts in pond
  • Lithosere = starts on bare rock
  • Ends at Climax community

🔺 Ecological Pyramids

  • Number: can be inverted
  • Biomass: can be inverted (aquatic)
  • Energy: ALWAYS upright
  • Producers at base; apex = top consumers

🔥 Unit 2 — One-liner Summary

Natural resources (renewable & non-renewable) support all life. Land resources face degradation through soil erosion, desertification, and mining — controlled by afforestation, terracing, check dams. Water resources are critical but threatened by unsustainable use, pollution, and over-extraction — managed via rainwater harvesting, watershed management. Forest resources provide protective, productive, regulative, and accessory functions — threatened by deforestation. Energy resources span renewable (solar, wind, hydro, biomass, tidal, geothermal) and non-renewable (fossil fuels, nuclear) sources. Ecosystem = biotic + abiotic components; food chains → food webs → ecological pyramids (energy always upright); ecological succession progresses from pioneer community to climax community.
