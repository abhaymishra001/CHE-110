Introduction to Biodiversity

What is Biodiversity?

The word Biodiversity = Bio (life) + Diversity (variety). It refers to the variability among living organisms from all sources — terrestrial, marine, and other aquatic ecosystems — and the ecological complexes of which they are part.

📖 Official Definition (CBD, 1992) "Biodiversity is the variability among living organisms from all sources including, inter alia, terrestrial, marine and other aquatic ecosystems and the ecological complexes of which they are part; this includes diversity within species, between species and of ecosystems." — Convention on Biological Diversity, Rio Earth Summit 1992

India's Ranking in Biodiversity

  • 🥇 10th among plant-rich countries of the world
  • 🥇 11th in terms of endemic species of higher vertebrates
  • 🥇 6th among centres of diversity and origin of agricultural crops
  • One of 18 mega-diversity nations identified by Conservation International (1998)

1. Genetic Diversity

It is the variation in DNA sequences among individuals of the same species. It provides the raw material for evolution and adaptation.

🐕 Classic Example Dogs (Canis lupus familiaris) — over 400 recognised breeds exist, ranging from tiny Chihuahuas to massive Great Danes. All belong to the same species but show enormous genetic variation in size, shape, behaviour, and physiology.

Why Genetic Diversity Matters

  • Helps species adapt to changing environments
  • Reduces risk of genetic diseases due to inbreeding
  • Forms the basis for crop improvement and disease-resistant varieties
  • Enables development of new medicines and biotechnology

Examples of Genetic Diversity

  • Rice (Oryza sativa) — thousands of varieties (Basmati, Sona Masoori, etc.)
  • Mango (Mangifera indica) — Alphonso, Langra, Totapuri, Dasheri, etc.
  • Human blood groups — A, B, AB, O varieties within one species

2. Species Diversity

It is the diversity of different species within a habitat or ecosystem. It can be measured as species richness (number of species), species abundance, and evenness.

🌿 Key Examples: Tropical Rainforest Amazon, Congo, Western Ghats — Mammals: jaguar, sloth, monkeys | Birds: parrots, toucans, hornbills | Reptiles & Amphibians: anacondas, poison dart frogs, chameleons | Insects: millions of beetle and butterfly species

Measuring Species Diversity

  • Alpha (α) diversity — local species richness within a single community
  • Beta (β) diversity — change in species composition between two communities
  • Gamma (γ) diversity — total diversity across a large region

The Shannon-Wiener Index (H') is commonly used to quantify species diversity: H' = −Σ(pᵢ × ln pᵢ), where pᵢ is the proportion of each species.

3. Ecosystem Diversity

It refers to the diversity of ecosystems found in a particular place or across the Earth, along with the ecological interactions among organisms and their physical environment.

Importance of Biodiversity

A. Ecosystem Services

Biodiversity supports ecosystem services that are vital for human survival and civilisation. These include:

Four Categories of Ecosystem Services (Millennium Ecosystem Assessment)
1. Provisioning — food, water, raw materials, medicine
2. Regulating — climate regulation, flood control, pollination
3. Cultural — recreation, aesthetic, spiritual
4. Supporting — nutrient cycling, soil formation, oxygen production
  • Air & Water Purification: Plants absorb CO₂, wetlands filter water
  • Pollination: ~75% of flowering plant species depend on animal pollinators; 35% of global food crop production depends on pollinators (bees, butterflies, birds)
  • Climate Regulation: Forests act as carbon sinks — Amazon stores ~150 billion tonnes of carbon
  • Soil Fertility: Earthworms, fungi, bacteria decompose organic matter, recycle nutrients
  • Nutrient Cycling: Nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon cycles maintained by diverse organisms

B. Economic Value

Food Resources

  • Biodiversity ensures a diverse range of food sources — crops, livestock, fish, wild foods
  • India has over 50,000 plant species with known economic uses

Raw Materials

  • Plants, animals, and microorganisms provide materials for fuel, clothing, building materials
  • Timber, bamboo, cotton, silk, rubber — all from biodiversity

Medicinal Resources

  • Over 25% of all modern medicines are derived from plants
  • Examples: Aspirin (willow bark), Morphine (opium poppy), Taxol (Pacific yew tree)
  • Traditional systems — Ayurveda, Unani, Siddha rely heavily on plant diversity

Tourism & Recreation (Ecotourism)

  • Ecotourism is a significant economic driver in biodiversity-rich regions
  • Contributes billions to national GDPs (e.g., Kaziranga, Corbett generate revenue)

Fisheries & Aquaculture

Healthy marine and freshwater ecosystems provide food and livelihoods for hundreds of millions worldwide.

C. Ecological Value

  • Ecosystem Functioning: Biodiversity supports nutrient cycling, soil formation, primary production
  • Stability & Resilience: More diverse ecosystems are more stable — if one species declines, others can fill the gap
  • Predator-Prey Balance: Wolves in Yellowstone — their reintroduction reshaped river courses ("trophic cascade")
  • Decomposition: Fungi and bacteria break down dead matter, releasing nutrients
🔑 Keystone Species Concept A species that has a disproportionately large effect on its ecosystem relative to its abundance. Example: Sea otters control sea urchin populations, which protects kelp forests.

D. Social Value

  • Cultural Significance: Many cultures around the world rely on biodiversity for food, medicine, and spiritual practices. Sacred groves (Devrais) in India protect local biodiversity.
  • Health & Well-being: Biodiversity contributes to human health by providing medicinal plants. "Nature therapy" or "forest bathing" (Shinrin-yoku) reduces stress and boosts immunity.
  • Indigenous Knowledge: Tribal communities hold rich traditional ecological knowledge about local biodiversity

E. Aesthetic Value

  • Natural Beauty: The diversity of plant and animal species creates stunning landscapes — from tropical rainforests and coral reefs to grasslands and deserts
  • Inspiration for Art: Nature has inspired art, literature, music, and other forms of cultural expression throughout history
  • Recreation: Bird watching, wildlife photography, nature trekking — multi-billion dollar industries

F. Informational Value

  • Scientific Discovery: Studying biodiversity helps scientists understand ecological processes, species interactions, and evolutionary patterns
  • Genetic & Evolutionary Information: Biodiversity holds critical genetic information usable in biotechnology — disease-resistant crops, new medicines, vaccines
  • Bioprospecting: Searching biodiversity for new compounds — e.g., Penicillin (fungus), Cyclosporin (immunosuppressant from soil fungus)
💡 Did You Know? The blood of horseshoe crabs (Limulus) contains a compound (LAL) used to test vaccines and IV drugs for bacterial contamination — a multi-billion dollar industry based on one species!

Hot Spots of Biodiversity

Definition & Criteria

A biodiversity hotspot is a biogeographic region with a significant reservoir of biodiversity that is under serious threat from humans.

⚠️ Three Criteria to identify a Hotspot (Norman Myers, 1988)
1. Must have at least 1,500 species of endemic vascular plants (≥ 0.5% of world total)
2. Must have lost at least 70% of its original habitat
3. High degree of threat from human activities

Currently there are 36 biodiversity hotspots worldwide (Conservation International updated list). They cover only ~2.4% of Earth's land surface but contain over 50% of endemic plant species.

Threats to Biodiversity

Major Threats (HIPPO)

🦛 HIPPO Framework — Major Causes of Biodiversity Loss
H — Habitat loss & destruction
I — Invasive species (biological invasions)
P — Pollution
P — Population (human) growth
O — Overexploitation (poaching)

1. Habitat Loss & Fragmentation

The most significant cause of biodiversity loss globally. Causes include deforestation, urbanisation, agricultural expansion, mining, dam construction.

  • ~13 million hectares of forest lost annually worldwide
  • Fragmentation isolates populations — reduces gene flow and increases inbreeding
  • India loses forest cover due to mining (especially in Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh)

2. Poaching of Wildlife

  • Illegal hunting and trade of wild animals for meat, traditional medicine, exotic pets
  • Tiger poaching for bones (traditional Chinese medicine); elephant poaching for ivory
  • Rhinoceros horn, sea turtle eggs, pangolin scales — all targeted by poachers
  • Blackbuck case: Bollywood actor Salman Khan sentenced to 5 years imprisonment for poaching blackbucks — highlights the issue
  • CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species) regulates wildlife trade

3. Biological Invasions

Introduction of alien/invasive species that outcompete native species, disrupt ecosystems.

  • Water Hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes) — covers water bodies, depletes oxygen, kills aquatic life
  • Lantana camara — invasive weed in Indian forests that outcompetes native vegetation
  • Nile Perch in Lake Victoria — caused extinction of 200+ cichlid fish species
  • Parthenium (Congress grass) — toxic invasive weed in India

4. Human-Wildlife Conflict

Encounters between humans and wildlife that lead to negative results — loss of property, livelihoods, and even life.

🐘 Examples from India
• Elephants kill ~500 people annually in India (especially Kerala)
• Corbett National Park (Uttarakhand) leopards responsible for 400+ attacks on humans
• Bengal tigers in Sundarbans kill 50–250 people per year in India and Bangladesh

Causes of Human-Wildlife Conflict

  • Agricultural expansion into forest areas
  • Deforestation — animals lose habitat, venture into human areas
  • Urbanisation — shrinking wildlife corridors
  • Transport networks cutting through forests (road-kills)
  • Lack of corridors — animals can't move between forest patches
  • Collection of timber and NTFPs (Non-Timber Forest Products)

1. Extinct Species

Species that no longer exist anywhere on Earth. The last individual has died — no possibility of recovery in wild or captivity. IUCN category: EX.

Famous Extinct Species

  • 🦕 Dinosaurs — mass extinction ~66 million years ago
  • 🐦 Dodo (Raphus cucullatus) — extinct 1681, Mauritius
  • 🐯 Tasmanian Tiger (Thylacinus cynocephalus) — extinct 1936
  • 🦣 Woolly Mammoth — extinct ~4,000 years ago
  • 🐸 Golden Toad — extinct 1989 due to climate change
  • 🕊️ Passenger Pigeon — extinct 1914; once billions existed!
  • Indian Cheetah (Acinonyx jubatus venaticus) — extinct in India by 1952
⚠️ Key Fact We are currently experiencing the 6th Mass Extinction — at a rate 100–1,000 times faster than natural background extinction rates, primarily driven by human activity.

2. Endangered Species

Plants, animals or other organisms at risk of becoming extinct. Risk arises from habitat loss, climate change, poaching, pollution, and invasive species. WWF and IUCN work to identify and protect these species.

3. Vulnerable Species

A species considered to be at high risk of extinction in the wild in the medium-term future if the causal factors continue to operate. IUCN category: VU.

Causes of Vulnerability

  • Habitat destruction (deforestation, urbanisation, mining)
  • Poaching and overexploitation
  • Climate change altering habitats
  • Pollution (water, air, soil)

Examples from India

  • 🐦 Sarus Crane (Grus antigone) — tallest flying bird; threatened by wetland loss
  • 🐒 Nilgiri Langur — found in Western Ghats
  • 🦌 Barasingha (Swamp Deer) — found in Kanha National Park
  • 🦁 Asiatic Lion — only ~700 individuals remaining in Gir Forest

4. Rare Species

Species that exists in small population numbers or is found in limited geographical areas, but is NOT currently endangered or vulnerable. They may become threatened if their habitat is disturbed, but at present they are relatively stable.

Key Difference: Rare vs Endangered

Rare species → small populations but NOT necessarily declining
Vulnerable/Endangered species → declining populations with real extinction risk

Causes of Rarity

  • Restricted habitat range (endemic to small area)
  • Low reproductive rates
  • Over-specialisation (dependence on particular food or conditions)
  • Human activities — deforestation, hunting, pollution

Examples in India

  • Nilgiri Tahr · Greater One-horned Rhinoceros · Asiatic Lion · Snow Leopard · Blackbuck

5. EDGE Species

EDGE = Evolutionarily Distinct and Globally Endangered. A framework developed to highlight conservation of species that are:

  • At high risk of extinction (globally endangered)
  • Possess unique evolutionary significance — represent isolated branches on the tree of life
🧬 Why EDGE matters If an EDGE species goes extinct, an entire unique branch of evolutionary history is lost forever — a loss far greater than losing a "non-distinct" species.

Examples of EDGE Species

  • 🐘 Asian Elephants — only two living genera of elephants; ancient lineage
  • 🦔 Chinese Pangolin — most trafficked mammal on Earth
  • 🐾 Malayan Tapir — odd-toed ungulate; unchanged for millions of years
  • 🦊 Red Panda — only living member of family Ailuridae

The Red Data Book

A document established by IUCN for documenting rare and endangered species of plants, animals, and fungi. Introduced in the late 1960s. The IUCN Red List of Threatened Species is the most well-known and widely used version globally.

Conservation of Biodiversity

Introduction

Wildlife conservation is the practice of protecting animal species and their habitats. There are two main approaches:

🌿 In-Situ Conservation

Protection of species in their natural habitat

  • National Parks
  • Wildlife Sanctuaries
  • Biosphere Reserves
  • Sacred Groves

🏛️ Ex-Situ Conservation

Protection in a place away from natural habitat

  • Zoos & Aquaria
  • Botanical Gardens
  • Gene Banks / Seed Banks
  • Tissue Culture

In-Situ Conservation

A. National Parks

  • Areas dedicated to conserve wild animals and natural scenery
  • All private rights are non-existent
  • Forestry operations, grazing of domestic animals — all prohibited
  • No human inhabitation except on-duty public servants & people permitted by Chief Wildlife Warden
  • Comprises the Core Zone only
  • Examples: Jim Corbett (India's first NP, 1936), Kaziranga, Ranthambore, Gir, Kanha

B. Wildlife Sanctuaries

  • Areas where it is illegal to interfere with natural life — hunting, shooting, fishing prohibited
  • Killing or capturing of any animal prohibited except under authority orders
  • Provides protection and optimum living conditions to wild animals
  • Boundary is NOT fixed by legislation (unlike national parks)
  • Comprises Core, Buffer & Restoration zones
  • Example: Vedanthangal Bird Sanctuary (TN), Chilika Sanctuary (Odisha)

C. Biosphere Reserves

Areas of terrestrial and coastal ecosystems promoting solutions to reconcile conservation of biodiversity with its sustainable use. Internationally recognised, nominated by national governments.

Zones of a Biosphere Reserve
TRANSITION ZONE — Human settlements, cropping, recreation, forestry
↕️
RESTORATION ZONE — Degraded areas being restored to natural state
↕️
BUFFER ZONE — Limited activities: research, education, eco-tourism
↕️
CORE ZONE — No human activity; legally protected, undisturbed ecosystem

India's Biosphere Reserves (18 total)

Examples: Nilgiri (1st in India, 1986), Sundarbans, Nanda Devi, Gulf of Mannar, Great Nicobar, Pachmarhi, Achanakmar-Amarkantak, Agasthyamala, etc.

Advantages of In-Situ Conservation

  • Ecological integrity is maintained and managed
  • Better opportunity for conservation as well as evolution
  • Cheaper way of conservation

Disadvantages

  • Less protection against pollution
  • Poachers and eco-tourists may cause damage

Ex-Situ Conservation

A. Zoos

  • Originally display facilities for public enjoyment & education
  • Modern zoos take on captive breeding programmes to increase populations
  • Example: Delhi Zoo, Mysore Zoo; captive breeding of Indian rhinoceros

B. Aquaria

  • Mainly used for threatened and endangered freshwater species
  • Also help in marine species conservation research

C. Botanical Gardens

  • Conservation of rare and endangered plant species
  • Study and research of plant characters; disseminate scientific information
  • Examples: Indian Botanical Garden (Howrah), Lalbagh (Bengaluru), Lloyd Botanical Garden (Darjeeling)

D. Gene Banks

  • Seed Bank — Seeds stored at low temperature & humidity for long periods
  • Pollen Bank — Pollen grains of plants cryopreserved
  • Sperm Bank — Animal reproductive cells stored cryogenically
  • Ova Bank — Egg cells stored for future use
  • Famous: National Bureau of Plant Genetic Resources (NBPGR), New Delhi; Svalbard Global Seed Vault (Norway)

E. Tissue Culture Technique

Asexual propagation in plants — a small piece of shoot apex, leaf section or individual cell is cut and placed in sterile culture in test tube/petri dish. Used to propagate rare and endangered plants rapidly.

F. DNA Technology

DNA of plant or animal cell is preserved. DNA technology provides an innovative approach for biodiversity conservation — extracting and storing genetic material of threatened species.

Advantages of Ex-Situ Conservation

  • Organisms completely protected from predation and poaching
  • Species survive longer and may breed more offspring than usual
  • Quality of offspring may be improved by genetic techniques
  • Breeding of hybrid species is possible

Disadvantages

  • Animals may not behave normally → reproduction difficult
  • Animals may not survive reintroduction into wild
  • Overprotection may result in loss of natural occurrence

India divided into 9 phytogeographical regions based on flora: Western Himalayas, Eastern Himalayas, Western Deserts, Gangetic Plains, Central India, Western Coast, Deccan Plateau, North-East India, Andaman & Nicobar Islands.
