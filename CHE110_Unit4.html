🌍 1 Introduction to Environmental Pollution

Pollution is the introduction of contaminants (harmful substances or energy) into the natural environment that cause adverse changes. Pollution can take the form of chemical substances or energy such as noise, heat, or light.

Think of it this way: Imagine your clean drinking water becoming yellowish and smelly because a factory is dumping chemicals into the river upstream — that's water pollution! Similarly, when vehicle smoke makes the air difficult to breathe — that's air pollution.

Pollutant = the agent or substance that causes pollution. It can be a foreign substance (like DDT, lead) or a naturally occurring substance in excess amounts (like CO₂).

Types of Pollutants

Based on Origin
  • Primary Pollutants – Released directly from source into environment (e.g., CO, SO₂, smoke, dust)
  • Secondary Pollutants – Formed by chemical reactions between primary pollutants (e.g., Ozone O₃, PAN, H₂SO₄)
Based on Degradability
  • Biodegradable – Can be broken down by microbes (e.g., vegetable waste, sewage)
  • Non-biodegradable – Cannot be broken down naturally (e.g., plastics, DDT, mercury)

💨 2 Air Pollution

Air Pollution is the presence of harmful substances (chemicals, particulate matter, biological materials) in the atmosphere at concentrations that endanger health or welfare of organisms, or damage the environment.

The National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) are set by the government to define the permissible levels of pollutants in outdoor air. When these limits are exceeded, health risks arise.

Types of Air Pollutants

Primary Pollutants (Direct Release)
  • Particulate matter: soil, lead, asbestos
  • Carbon oxides: CO, CO₂
  • Sulphur dioxide: SO₂
  • Nitrogen oxides: NO, NO₂
  • Hydrocarbons: Methane (CH₄), Benzene
  • Ammonia (NH₃)
Secondary Pollutants (Formed in Atmosphere)
  • Ozone (O₃) — Photochemical smog
  • Sulphur trioxide (SO₃)
  • Nitric acid (HNO₃)
  • Sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄)
  • PAN — Peroxy Acetyl Nitrate
  • Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)

Suspended Particulate Matter (SPM)

SPM refers to tiny solid particles or liquid droplets suspended in air, varying from large dust particles to fine aerosols.

PM₁₀ (Coarse particles)
  • Diameter < 10 micrometers
  • Can enter lungs
  • Sources: dust, pollen, sea spray
PM₂.₅ (Fine particles)
  • Diameter < 2.5 micrometers
  • Can enter bloodstream
  • Sources: combustion, smoke, soot
⚠️ PM₂.₅ is more dangerous than PM₁₀ because it penetrates deeper into the respiratory system and can pass into the blood!

Causes of Air Pollution

🌋 Natural Causes
  • Volcanic eruptions → release SO₂, ash
  • Forest fires → CO, smoke particles
  • Cyclones and dust storms
  • Marsh gas (CH₄) from decaying matter
  • Natural emissions from animals
🏭 Anthropogenic (Man-made) Causes
  • Industrial emissions
  • Automobile exhaust
  • Thermal power stations
  • Nuclear explosions & power plants
  • Agricultural activities (fertilizers)
  • Mining activities
  • Disposal of garbage (open burning)

Effects of Air Pollution

  • Human Health: Respiratory diseases (asthma, bronchitis), cardiovascular diseases, cancer (lung), neurological damage
  • Plants: Chlorosis (yellowing), necrosis (tissue death), reduced photosynthesis, crop damage
  • Soil: Acidification through acid rain, reduced fertility
  • Buildings: Corrosion of metals, blackening of marble (like Taj Mahal!)
  • Climate: Global warming, ozone depletion, climate change
  • Visibility: Smog reduces visibility → traffic accidents
🔴 Remember: Taj Mahal is turning yellow/black due to SO₂ and other air pollutants from the Mathura refinery — this is called "Marble Cancer"!

Control of Air Pollution

  • Use of clean fuels like CNG, LPG, electric vehicles
  • Catalytic converters in vehicles to reduce exhaust emissions
  • Planting trees and green belts in urban areas
  • Air pollution control devices in industries
  • Strict implementation of Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981
  • Public awareness and use of public transport
  • Renewable energy (solar, wind) instead of coal

💧 3 Water Pollution

Water Pollution is the addition of various organic and inorganic substances that change the physical and chemical properties of water, thereby leading to detrimental effects on living organisms and reducing water usability.

Globally, about 2.2 billion people lack access to safe drinking water. Water pollution is a major reason for this crisis.

Sources & Causes of Water Pollution

Point Sources

(Identifiable, specific locations)

  • Industrial effluents (chemicals, heavy metals)
  • Sewage treatment plants overflow
  • Oil spills from ships
  • Mining drainage
Non-Point Sources

(Diffuse, cannot be traced to one place)

  • Agricultural runoff (fertilizers, pesticides)
  • Urban stormwater runoff
  • Acid deposition
  • Atmospheric fallout
Major Causes:
  • Industrial Waste: Chemicals, heavy metals discharged into rivers
  • Sewage & Domestic Waste: Untreated household waste polluting water bodies
  • Agricultural Runoff: Fertilizers (nitrates, phosphates) and pesticides contaminating water
  • Oil Spills: From ships polluting oceans and seas
  • Plastic Waste: Dumping of garbage into rivers and lakes
  • Thermal & Radioactive Waste: From power plants raising water temperature
  • Religious Rituals: Idol immersion, dead body disposal in rivers

BOD is the amount of dissolved oxygen needed by microorganisms to break down organic material in a given water sample. Higher BOD = More polluted water!

Enrichment of a standing water body (lake/pond) by nutrients (phosphorus, nitrogen) leading to excessive algae growth.

Marine Pollution

Oceans cover 71% of Earth but are increasingly polluted from land-based and sea-based sources.

  • Sources: Rivers (carrying land pollutants), oil drilling, shipment accidents, coastal runoff
  • Pollutants: Sewage sludge, industrial effluents, synthetic detergents, agrochemicals, plastics, metals, waste heat
  • Great Pacific Garbage Patch: Massive accumulation of plastic debris in the Pacific Ocean

🌱 4 Soil Pollution (Land Pollution)

Soil Pollution (also called Land Pollution) refers to the degradation of Earth's land surface through misuse of soil, contamination with pesticides, industrial wastes, and other harmful substances, resulting in loss of fertile topsoil and adverse effects on organisms.

Causes of Soil Pollution

🧪
Chemical Fertilizers
Excessive use of synthetic fertilizers alters soil chemistry
🐛
Pesticides/Herbicides
DDT, BHC persist in soil and kill beneficial organisms
🏭
Industrial Waste
Heavy metals (Pb, Cd, Cr) make soil toxic and infertile
🗑️
Solid Waste Dumping
Landfills leach pollutants into soil and groundwater
☢️
Radioactive Waste
Nuclear fallout makes soil sterile for decades
🏘️
Urbanization
Construction and waste disposal degrade natural soil

Effects of Soil Pollution

  • Agricultural Impact: Reduced crop yield, affects food quality through absorption of heavy metals
  • Groundwater Contamination: Pollutants leach into underground water tables
  • Soil Microorganisms: Pesticides kill beneficial bacteria, fungi, earthworms → reduces fertility
  • Human Health: Skin diseases, cancer from contaminated crops and water
  • Ecological Impact: Loss of biodiversity, food chain disruption through bioaccumulation
  • Desertification: Misuse of land causes soil to become barren desert

Control of Soil Pollution

  • Use of Organic Manure: Replace chemical fertilizers with compost and bio-fertilizers (vermicompost)
  • Proper Waste Disposal: Treat industrial and household waste before dumping
  • Reduce Pesticide Use: Use eco-friendly and biodegradable pesticides, biological pest control
  • Recycling of Waste: Recycle plastics, metals, and glass to reduce soil contamination
  • Afforestation: Plant trees to prevent soil erosion and improve quality
  • Phytoremediation: Using plants (like sunflower, Indian mustard) to absorb and remove heavy metals
  • Bioremediation: Using microorganisms to degrade soil pollutants
  • Public Awareness: Educate people about harmful effects of soil pollution

🔊 5 Noise Pollution

Noise Pollution is any unwanted, unpleasant, or annoying sound that disrupts the natural balance and causes harm to humans and wildlife. Sound above 85 dB for prolonged periods causes hearing damage. Normal conversation = 60 dB; Jet plane = 140 dB.

Causes of Noise Pollution

🏙️ Urban Sources
  • Traffic noise (cars, bikes, trucks)
  • Construction activities
  • Industrial machinery
  • Railways & airports
  • Loudspeakers at public events
  • Firecrackers & celebrations
🏠 Household Sources
  • Music systems & televisions
  • Air conditioners
  • Generators & pumps
  • Household appliances
  • Social gatherings

Noise Levels Chart

Sound SourceLevel (dB)Effect
Whisper30 dBVery soft
Normal conversation60 dBComfortable
City traffic70–85 dBAnnoying
Factory machinery90–100 dBHearing damage with prolonged exposure
Rock concert110–120 dBPain threshold approached
Jet engine / Firecrackers130–140 dBImmediate hearing damage, pain

Effects of Noise Pollution

  • Auditory (Hearing): Temporary hearing loss → permanent deafness (NIHL – Noise-Induced Hearing Loss)
  • Psychological: Stress, anxiety, irritability, depression, sleep disturbance
  • Physiological: High blood pressure (hypertension), cardiovascular disease, hormonal imbalances
  • Cognitive: Reduced concentration, poor academic performance (in children near airports/roads)
  • Wildlife: Disrupts animal communication, navigation (dolphins, whales), migratory patterns
⚠️ NIHL (Noise-Induced Hearing Loss) is the most common occupational health problem worldwide — affects millions of factory workers!

Control of Noise Pollution

  • Low-Noise Machinery: Promote modern quieter machines in factories
  • Sound Barriers: Noise walls along highways, sound insulation in buildings
  • Green Belts: Planting trees alongside roads reduces noise levels
  • Zoning: Separate residential, industrial, and commercial zones
  • Control on Loudspeakers: Limit use especially at night (Noise Pollution Rules, 2000)
  • Personal Protection: Earplugs or earmuffs in high-noise workplaces
  • Regular Maintenance: Well-maintained vehicles and machines produce less noise
  • Legislation: Enforce laws and standards for permissible noise levels

☢️ 6 Radiation Pollution

Radiation Pollution refers to the release of radioactive substances or exposure to high levels of ionizing radiation from natural or artificial sources that adversely affect living organisms and the environment.

Ionizing electromagnetic radiation used in medicine & industry

Sources of Radiation Pollution

Natural Sources
  • Cosmic rays from outer space
  • Radon gas from rocks/soil
  • Radioactive elements in soil (uranium, thorium)
  • Radioactive potassium in the body
Artificial Sources
  • Nuclear power plants
  • Nuclear weapons testing
  • Medical X-rays & nuclear medicine
  • Industrial uses (gamma radiography)
  • Radioactive waste disposal
  • Nuclear accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima)

Effects of Radiation Pollution

  • Acute Effects: Radiation sickness (nausea, vomiting, hair loss), burns, death
  • Chronic Effects: Cancer (leukemia, thyroid cancer), genetic mutations
  • Reproductive Effects: Sterility, mutations in offspring, birth defects
  • Immune System: Weakening of immune system → vulnerability to infections
  • Genetic Effects: DNA damage passed to future generations (heritable mutations)
💡 Chernobyl (1986, Ukraine) & Fukushima (2011, Japan) are the two worst nuclear accidents. Hiroshima & Nagasaki atomic bombs (1945) showed radiation's devastating effects on humans!

Control of Radiation Pollution

  • Proper shielding (lead, concrete) around radioactive materials
  • Safe disposal of radioactive waste in deep geological repositories
  • Regular monitoring of radiation levels
  • International treaties: Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)
  • Strict regulations on nuclear plant operation
  • Use of protective equipment (lead aprons in hospitals)

🆕 7 Emerging Pollutants

Emerging Pollutants (also called Contaminants of Emerging Concern – CECs) are newly identified or previously unrecognized contaminants found in the environment that can pose risks to human health and ecosystems but are not yet regulated or fully studied.

As technology advances and we develop new chemicals and materials, new types of pollutants emerge that our existing regulatory frameworks have not yet addressed.

Types of Emerging Pollutants

Microplastics — A Special Concern

Microplastics are plastic particles smaller than 5 mm. They come from:

  • Breakdown of larger plastic waste
  • Microbeads in personal care products
  • Synthetic clothing fibers (polyester, nylon)
⚠️ Scientists have found microplastics in human blood, lungs, placentas, and even in the summit of Mount Everest! They are now virtually everywhere on Earth.

🌡️ 9 Global Warming & Climate Change

Global Warming refers to the gradual increase in Earth's average surface temperature due to the excessive buildup of Greenhouse Gases (GHGs) in the atmosphere, primarily caused by human activities.
Climate Change is the broader term for long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While natural, climate change since the mid-20th century is primarily caused by human activities, especially burning of fossil fuels.

The Greenhouse Effect

The Greenhouse Effect is a natural process: the Sun's radiation passes through the atmosphere, warms Earth's surface, and when Earth radiates this heat back, certain gases (GHGs) trap some of it — keeping Earth warm (about 15°C). Without it, Earth would be -18°C!

The Enhanced Greenhouse Effect (the problem): Human activities increase GHG concentrations → more heat trapped → Earth warms beyond natural levels.

Effects of Global Warming

  • Melting Ice Caps & Glaciers: Arctic, Antarctic ice & Himalayan glaciers melting → sea level rise
  • Sea Level Rise: Threatens coastal cities like Mumbai, Kolkata, Bangladesh
  • Extreme Weather: More frequent and intense cyclones, droughts, floods, heat waves
  • Biodiversity Loss: Species unable to adapt to rapid temperature changes go extinct
  • Agricultural Impact: Changed rainfall patterns → reduced crop yields, food insecurity
  • Ocean Acidification: CO₂ dissolves in seawater → carbonic acid → coral bleaching
  • Human Health: Spread of tropical diseases (malaria, dengue) to new areas
  • Economic Impacts: Damage to infrastructure, reduced productivity, higher costs

Control Measures

Mitigation (Reducing GHGs)
  • Renewable energy (solar, wind, hydro)
  • Energy efficiency improvements
  • Reforestation and afforestation
  • Carbon capture technology
  • Electric vehicles
Adaptation (Dealing with effects)
  • Sea walls & flood defenses
  • Drought-resistant crop varieties
  • Early warning systems
  • Relocating vulnerable communities
  • Water conservation
✅ International Agreements: Paris Agreement (2015) — 196 countries pledged to limit warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels. Kyoto Protocol (1997) was the first international treaty on GHG reduction.

🌐 10 Ozone Layer Depletion

The Ozone Layer (Ozonosphere) is a region of Earth's stratosphere (15–35 km altitude) containing high concentrations of ozone (O₃) that absorbs 97–99% of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation.
Ozone Layer Depletion is the thinning of the ozone layer in the stratosphere, primarily caused by man-made chemicals called Ozone Depleting Substances (ODS), mainly Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).

Ozone-Depleting Substances (ODS)

❄️
CFCs
Chlorofluorocarbons — in refrigerators, ACs, aerosol sprays
🔥
Halons
Used in fire extinguishers; very potent ODS
🌾
Methyl Bromide
Used as pesticide in agriculture; attacks ozone
🧹
Carbon Tetrachloride
Industrial solvent and dry cleaning agent

Mechanism of Ozone Depletion

CFCs are released → rise into stratosphere → UV radiation breaks CFC → releases chlorine atom (Cl) → Cl attacks ozone.

Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂  |  ClO + O → Cl + O₂  |  Net: O₃ + O → 2O₂
⚠️ One Cl atom can destroy 100,000 ozone molecules because Cl is regenerated in the reaction — it acts as a catalyst!

The famous Ozone Hole over Antarctica was first discovered in 1985. It forms each spring when UV light breaks up CFCs and chlorine destroys ozone in very cold polar stratospheric clouds.

Effects of Ozone Depletion

  • Human Health: Increased skin cancer (melanoma), cataracts, immune system suppression
  • Plants: Reduced photosynthesis, crop damage (soybeans, wheat sensitive to UV)
  • Marine Ecosystem: Damage to phytoplankton (base of ocean food chain)
  • Climate: UV-B affects climate patterns; ozone itself is a GHG in stratosphere
  • Materials: UV degrades paints, rubber, plastics faster

Control Measures

✅ Montreal Protocol (1987): The most successful international environmental agreement! 197 countries agreed to phase out ODS. CFCs have been replaced by HFCs (hydrofluorocarbons) in refrigeration. The ozone hole is slowly healing!
  • Phase out production and use of CFCs, halons, and other ODS
  • Switch to CFC-free refrigerants (HFCs, natural refrigerants)
  • Regular monitoring of stratospheric ozone levels
  • Proper disposal of old refrigerators and ACs

🌧️ 11 Acid Rain

Acid Rain is any form of precipitation (rain, snow, fog, hail) with a pH lower than 5.6 (normal rain pH = 5.6 due to dissolved CO₂ forming carbonic acid H₂CO₃). It is caused by SO₂ and NOₓ emissions reacting with water in the atmosphere.

Formation of Acid Rain

SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ (Sulphurous acid) | 2SO₂ + O₂ → 2SO₃ | SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (Sulphuric acid)
2NO₂ + H₂O → HNO₃ (Nitric acid) + HNO₂ (Nitrous acid)

These acids dissolve in rainwater, forming acid rain. The pH can drop as low as 2–4 in severely polluted areas!

Effects of Acid Rain

Effects on Human Communities
  • Contaminates drinking water sources
  • Leaches toxic metals (Al, Pb) into water
  • Damages buildings, bridges, historical monuments
  • Respiratory problems from acid aerosols
  • Economic losses to fisheries and tourism
Effects on Agriculture
  • Soil acidification → leaching of essential nutrients (Ca, Mg)
  • Damage to leaves, wax coating
  • Reduced crop yields
  • Killing of fish & aquatic organisms
  • "Forest die-back" — killing of trees
💡 The Taj Mahal in Agra is a victim of acid rain! SO₂ from Mathura refinery reacts with marble (CaCO₃) → gypsum (CaSO₄) → the white marble turns yellow/black. This is called "Marble Cancer"!

Control of Acid Rain

  • Reduce SO₂ emissions: use low-sulphur coal, FGD (Flue Gas Desulphurization) systems in power plants
  • Reduce NOₓ emissions: catalytic converters in vehicles, low-NOₓ burners
  • Switch to renewable energy (solar, wind) — no SO₂/NOₓ produced
  • Liming of acidified lakes and soils to neutralize acidity
  • International cooperation (acid rain crosses national boundaries)

📋 12 Case Studies on Pollution

1. 🏙️ Delhi Air Pollution Crisis

Delhi consistently ranks among the most polluted cities globally. In winter (Oct–Jan), air quality reaches "Hazardous" levels (AQI > 400).

  • Causes: Stubble burning in Punjab/Haryana, vehicular emissions, construction dust, industrial pollution, temperature inversion (cold air trapping pollutants)
  • Effects: ~30,000 premature deaths annually attributed to air pollution in Delhi; schools close, outdoor activities banned
  • Measures: Odd-even vehicle scheme, GRAP (Graded Response Action Plan), BS-VI fuel standards
2. 🌊 Ganga River Pollution

The Ganga — India's most sacred river — is also one of the most polluted rivers in the world.

  • Causes: Untreated sewage from 29 cities along its banks, industrial effluents (especially from tanneries in Kanpur), religious offerings, dead body disposal
  • Coliform bacteria levels are thousands of times above safe limits at many points
  • Namami Gange Programme: ₹20,000 crore government initiative for Ganga cleaning (2015–2024)
3. ☢️ Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984)

The world's worst industrial disaster occurred on December 3, 1984, at the Union Carbide pesticide plant in Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh.

  • Cause: Accidental release of 40 tonnes of Methyl Isocyanate (MIC) gas
  • Immediate Deaths: 2,259 officially; estimates up to 15,000+
  • Long-term Effects: 500,000+ people exposed; continued health problems, genetic effects in subsequent generations
  • Lesson: Need for strict industrial safety standards and disaster preparedness
4. 🐟 Minamata Disease, Japan (1956)

Chisso Corporation discharged methylmercury into Minamata Bay for decades. Local fishermen and their families who ate contaminated fish developed severe neurological symptoms. Over 1,700 deaths; 10,000+ affected. Led to international awareness about industrial water pollution.

♻️ 13 Solid Waste Management

Solid Waste refers to any garbage, refuse, or sludge from a wastewater treatment plant, water supply treatment plant, or air pollution control facility, and other discarded material that is solid, liquid, semi-solid, or contained gaseous material.

India generates about 62 million tonnes of solid waste per year. Improper management causes pollution of air, water, and soil, and creates health hazards.

Control Measures for Urban Solid Waste

  • Segregation at Source: Separate biodegradable (green bin), non-biodegradable (blue bin), and hazardous (red bin) at household/institutional level
  • Collection & Transportation: Covered bins and vehicles, door-to-door collection to prevent littering
  • Composting: Biodegradable waste (food scraps, leaves) converted to organic manure via aerobic/anaerobic process → vermicomposting
  • Recycling & Reuse: Recovery of paper, plastic, glass, metals from waste to reduce landfill burden
  • Incineration: Burning waste at high temperatures (850°C+) to reduce volume; generates energy (Waste-to-Energy plants)
  • Sanitary Landfill: Engineered disposal sites with liners to prevent leaching, gas collection systems
  • Bioremediation: Using microorganisms to degrade organic waste

Control Measures for Industrial Waste

  • Effluent Treatment Plants (ETP): Treat liquid industrial waste before discharge
  • Waste Minimization: Cleaner production processes to reduce waste at source
  • Stabilization/Solidification: Converting hazardous waste into stable, less harmful form
  • Secure Landfill: Special containment facilities for hazardous industrial waste
  • Deep Well Injection: Pumping liquid waste into deep underground formations (controversial)
  • Compliance with Hazardous Waste Management Rules, 2016

📖 Unit 4 Summary

🔑

Pollution Definition

Introduction of contaminants causing adverse changes. Types: Air, Water, Soil, Noise, Radiation. Primary pollutants released directly; secondary formed by reactions.

💨

Air Pollution

CO, SO₂, NOₓ, PM are key pollutants. Control via clean fuels, ESP, catalytic converters, Air Act 1981. Causes smog, acid rain, GHG effects.

💧

Water Pollution

BOD, eutrophication, biomagnification are key concepts. Three-stage treatment (primary → secondary → tertiary). Key laws: Water Act 1974.

🌱

Soil Pollution

DDT, heavy metals, excess fertilizers cause soil degradation. Solutions: phytoremediation, bioremediation, organic farming, recycling.

🌡️

Global Warming

Enhanced greenhouse effect by CO₂, CH₄, N₂O. Effects: sea level rise, extreme weather, biodiversity loss. Paris Agreement: 1.5°C limit.

🌐

Ozone Depletion

CFCs destroy ozone: 1 Cl atom = 100,000 O₃ molecules destroyed. Ozone hole over Antarctica. Montreal Protocol (1987) = solution.

🌧️

Acid Rain

pH < 5.6. SO₂ + NOₓ + water = H₂SO₄ + HNO₃. Damages marble, kills fish, harms crops. Taj Mahal victim of "Marble Cancer".

♻️

Solid Waste

62 MT/year in India. 3R principle: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle. Methods: composting, incineration, sanitary landfill. SWM Rules 2016.

Easy Q10
The 3R principle of waste management stands for:

Easy Q13
Carbon monoxide (CO) is dangerous because it:

Easy Q16
Photochemical smog is produced in the presence of:

Easy Q17
Which waste management method involves burning waste at high temperatures?

Incineration is the controlled combustion of waste at high temperatures (850°C+) to reduce volume and generate energy, though it can produce harmful gases if not properly managed.
Easy Q18
Acid rain is primarily caused by emissions of:

Easy Q19
What type of radiation has the highest penetrating power?

Gamma (γ) radiation has the highest penetrating power and requires thick lead or concrete shielding. Alpha is stopped by paper, beta by 3mm aluminum, but gamma needs heavy shielding.
Easy Q20

Microplastics (plastic particles < 5mm) are emerging pollutants found everywhere — in oceans, mountains, human blood, and placentas. They are not yet regulated and pose serious health risks.
Medium Q21

Electrostatic precipitators operate at 12–15 kV high voltage, charging particles that then get attracted to grounded collecting plates, removing them from the gas stream.
Medium Q22
Itai-itai disease is caused by contamination of water/food with:

Medium Q23
PAN (Peroxy Acetyl Nitrate) is formed during:
✅ Answer: B
PAN is a secondary pollutant formed in photochemical smog when NOₓ and hydrocarbons from vehicle exhaust undergo photochemical reactions in sunlight. It causes eye irritation and damages plants.
Medium Q24
What is the global warming potential of methane (CH₄) compared to CO₂?
✅ Answer: B
CH₄ has a Global Warming Potential of 28 over 100 years, meaning it is 28 times more effective at trapping heat than CO₂. N₂O has GWP of 265.
Medium Q25
Biomagnification refers to the process where:
✅ Answer: B
Biomagnification (bioaccumulation) means the concentration of persistent toxins like DDT, mercury increases at each trophic level. Top predators (including humans) accumulate the highest concentrations.
Medium Q26
The chemical formula for Peroxy Acetyl Nitrate (PAN) indicates it contains which elements?
✅ Answer: B
PAN (CH₃CO-O₂-NO₂) contains Carbon, Hydrogen, Nitrogen, and Oxygen. It's a secondary pollutant in photochemical smog, causing eye irritation and plant damage.
Medium Q27
Which stage of wastewater treatment involves trickling filters and activated sludge process?
✅ Answer: B
Secondary treatment uses biological processes including trickling filters, activated sludge process, and rotating biological contactors to remove dissolved organic matter from wastewater.
Medium Q28
Marble Cancer of the Taj Mahal is caused by which acid?
✅ Answer: B
H₂SO₄ in acid rain reacts with marble (CaCO₃) → CaSO₄ (gypsum) which is soft and discolored. CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂. This is the chemical reaction behind "Marble Cancer" affecting the Taj Mahal.
Medium Q29
Which of the following is NOT an effect of global warming?
✅ Answer: C
Healing of the ozone layer is due to the Montreal Protocol (banning CFCs), NOT an effect of global warming. Global warming effects include glacier melting, sea level rise, extreme weather, and ocean acidification.
Medium Q30
The Paris Agreement (2015) aims to limit global temperature rise to:
✅ Answer: B
The Paris Agreement (2015) committed 196 countries to limit global temperature rise to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to limit to 1.5°C.
Medium Q31
Which pollutant in cigarette smoke causes the MOST harm to cardiovascular system?
✅ Answer: A
CO in cigarette smoke (2-5% concentration) binds to hemoglobin forming carboxyhemoglobin, reducing oxygen delivery to heart and other tissues, directly contributing to cardiovascular disease.
Medium Q32
What does the AQI (Air Quality Index) value above 300 indicate?
✅ Answer: D
AQI 0–50 = Good, 51–100 = Satisfactory, 101–200 = Moderate, 201–300 = Poor, 301–400 = Very Poor, 401–500 = Severe/Hazardous. Delhi often crosses 400+ in winter.
Medium Q33
In anaerobic wastewater treatment, the FINAL step is:
✅ Answer: D
Anaerobic digestion sequence: Hydrolysis → Acidogenesis → Acetogenesis → Methanogenesis (production of methane CH₄). The biogas (methane) produced can be used as fuel.
Medium Q34
Fluorosis is caused by excess intake of which element in drinking water?
✅ Answer: B
Fluorosis is caused by excess fluoride in drinking water (> 1.5 mg/L WHO limit). It causes dental fluorosis (mottled teeth) and skeletal fluorosis (bone deformities). Common in Rajasthan and Andhra Pradesh.
Medium Q35
Which of these is a "persistent organic pollutant" (POP)?
✅ Answer: A
DDT is a classic persistent organic pollutant (POP) that resists biodegradation, bioaccumulates in food chains, and can travel long distances. Stockholm Convention (2001) banned 12 POPs including DDT.
Medium Q36
Temperature inversion in atmosphere is related to which type of pollution?
✅ Answer: A
Temperature inversion occurs when a layer of warm air sits above cooler air near ground, preventing normal convective mixing. This traps pollutants at ground level, worsening smog. This is why Delhi's pollution is worst in winter mornings.
Medium Q37
The process of using plants to clean up heavy metal contaminated soil is called:
✅ Answer: B
Phytoremediation uses plants like sunflower (Helianthus annuus), Indian mustard (Brassica juncea), and vetiver grass to absorb, contain, and/or degrade heavy metals from contaminated soil.
Medium Q38
Which of the following correctly describes "sanitary landfill"?
✅ Answer: B
Sanitary landfill is an engineered disposal method with clay/plastic liners to prevent leachate contaminating groundwater, gas collection pipes for biogas, and daily soil cover on waste layers. Far safer than open dumps.
Medium Q39
Endrin, Aldrin, and Dieldrin are examples of:
✅ Answer: B
Endrin, Aldrin, and Dieldrin are among the "Dirty Dozen" persistent organic pollutants (POPs) banned under the Stockholm Convention. They are organochlorine pesticides that persist in the environment and bioaccumulate.
Medium Q40
SPM stands for what in the context of air pollution?
✅ Answer: B
SPM (Suspended Particulate Matter) refers to tiny solid particles or liquid droplets suspended in the air. TSPM (Total SPM) includes both PM₁₀ and PM₂.₅ fractions.
Medium Q41
PFAS (Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances) are called "forever chemicals" because they:
✅ Answer: B
PFAS are called "forever chemicals" because the strong C-F bond makes them virtually indestructible in natural environments. They persist for thousands of years, accumulate in organisms, and have been found in human blood worldwide.
Medium Q42
Which industrial process releases barium compounds that can produce green color in fireworks while being toxic?
✅ Answer: A
Barium salts (like barium chlorate and barium nitrate) are burned in fireworks to produce green color. They are poisonous, affecting heart muscles and the nervous system when inhaled.
Medium Q43
The "Dirty Dozen" refers to a group of chemicals banned under which convention?
✅ Answer: B
The Stockholm Convention (2001) banned the "Dirty Dozen" — 12 of the most toxic persistent organic pollutants (POPs) including DDT, PCBs, dioxins, and furans.
Medium Q44
Which of these is the CORRECT sequence for solid waste management hierarchy (most preferred to least)?
✅ Answer: B
The waste management hierarchy: Reduce (best — prevent generation) → Reuse → Recycle → Energy recovery (incineration) → Disposal (landfill — least preferred). Prevention is always better than cure!
Medium Q45
Which type of radiation exposure causes DNA damage passed to future generations?
✅ Answer: B
Genetic effects of radiation affect the reproductive cells (gametes), causing mutations that can be passed to offspring. This is distinct from somatic effects which affect the irradiated individual only.
Hard Q46
The chemical reaction in an Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP) involves which fundamental principle?
✅ Answer: A
ESPs work by: (1) Corona discharge from high-voltage electrode ionizes surrounding gas, (2) ions attach to dust particles charging them, (3) charged particles migrate to grounded collection plates via electrostatic force (Coulomb's law), (4) collected particles periodically knocked off by rapping.
Hard Q47
In the ozone depletion cycle, what role does chlorine (Cl) play in the reaction Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂, followed by ClO + O → Cl + O₂?
✅ Answer: B
Cl acts as a catalyst — it's consumed in Step 1 but regenerated in Step 2. The net reaction is: O₃ + O → 2O₂. Since Cl is regenerated, one Cl atom can catalytically destroy over 100,000 ozone molecules before being deactivated.
Hard Q48
Methanogenesis in anaerobic digestion is carried out by which microorganisms and what is the primary product?
✅ Answer: B
Methanogenesis is performed by strictly anaerobic archaebacteria (methanogens like Methanobacterium, Methanosaeta). They convert acetate and H₂/CO₂ into methane (CH₄) and CO₂. The biogas produced contains ~60-70% CH₄ and is used as fuel.
Hard Q49
Which phenomenon explains why bird eggs became thinner and more fragile in areas exposed to DDT contamination?
✅ Answer: B
DDT metabolite (DDE) inhibits carbonic anhydrase enzyme in birds, which is essential for converting CO₂ to carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻) needed for CaCO₃ eggshell formation. This caused eggshell thinning and reproductive failure, nearly driving Bald Eagles and Peregrine Falcons to extinction in the US.
Hard Q50
In the context of acid rain, what is "dry deposition" as opposed to "wet deposition"?
✅ Answer: B
Wet deposition = acid gases dissolve in rain/snow and fall. Dry deposition = SO₂, NOₓ, and acid particles settle directly onto land, water, and vegetation surfaces without precipitation. Both cause acidification but dry deposition can cause more localized, severe damage near emission sources.
Hard Q51
The concept of "ocean acidification" in the context of global warming occurs because:
✅ Answer: B
Oceans absorb ~30% of CO₂ emissions. CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻, lowering ocean pH (currently ~8.1, down from 8.2 pre-industrial). This threatens corals (CaCO₃ dissolves in acid), mollusks, and the entire marine food web.
Hard Q52
Which factor distinguishes a "point source" from a "non-point source" of water pollution?
✅ Answer: B
Point source pollution comes from a specific, identifiable source (factory pipe, sewage outlet) — easier to regulate. Non-point source (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater) comes from large, diffuse areas — much harder to control and currently the leading cause of water quality problems globally.
Hard Q53
The "Blue Baby Syndrome" (methemoglobinemia) in infants is caused by high levels of which pollutant in drinking water?
✅ Answer: C
High nitrate (NO₃⁻) levels in drinking water (>50 mg/L) cause Blue Baby Syndrome in infants. Gut bacteria convert nitrate to nitrite, which converts hemoglobin to methemoglobin (cannot carry O₂), turning babies blue (cyanosis). Cause: agricultural fertilizer runoff.
Hard Q54
Why does the Antarctic ozone hole form specifically in spring (September-November) rather than year-round?
✅ Answer: B
In Antarctic winter, polar vortex isolates stratospheric air. Very low temperatures (-78°C) form Polar Stratospheric Clouds (PSCs). Heterogeneous reactions on PSC surfaces convert reservoir chlorine compounds (ClONO₂, HCl) to active Cl₂. When spring sunlight returns, Cl₂ photolyzes → Cl radicals rapidly destroy ozone.
Hard Q55
Radiocesium (¹³⁷Cs) released during nuclear accidents is particularly dangerous because:
✅ Answer: B
¹³⁷Cs (cesium-137) has a 30.17 year half-life, meaning contaminated areas remain radioactive for centuries. It behaves like potassium in the body, distributing into soft tissue and muscles, delivering continuous internal beta and gamma radiation, causing cancer.
Hard Q56
What is the mechanism by which barium in fireworks causes toxicity to the cardiovascular system?
✅ Answer: B
Soluble barium salts block K⁺ channels in cell membranes (hypokalemia = low blood potassium). This disrupts electrical signaling in heart muscle causing cardiac arrhythmias, ventricular fibrillation, and can be fatal. BaSO₄ (barium sulfate) is non-toxic as it's insoluble — used as X-ray contrast agent.
Hard Q57
The "activated sludge process" in secondary wastewater treatment achieves pollutant removal primarily through:
✅ Answer: B
In activated sludge, wastewater is aerated in a tank with concentrated microbial communities (the "activated sludge"). Microbes oxidize dissolved organic matter → CO₂ + H₂O + new biomass. The sludge settles in secondary clarifier; some is recycled back to maintain microbial population. Typical BOD removal: 85-95%.
Hard Q58
Why are endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) particularly harmful even at very low concentrations?
✅ Answer: B
Natural hormones function at nanomolar to picomolar concentrations (billionths to trillionths of a gram). EDCs (like BPA, phthalates, PCBs, estrogens from pharmaceuticals) can mimic or antagonize these signals at equally tiny concentrations — far below what traditional toxicology considered dangerous. This is why standard "dose-response" models may underestimate EDC risks.
Hard Q59
The concept of "cradle-to-grave" management applies specifically to which type of waste under Indian law?
✅ Answer: B
"Cradle-to-grave" means tracking hazardous waste from its point of generation through transportation, storage, treatment, and final disposal. India's Hazardous Waste Management Rules (2016) require manifest documentation at each stage, ensuring accountability. Generators, transporters, and disposers all have legal responsibilities.
Hard Q60
In terms of global warming potential (GWP) over 100 years, which correctly orders these gases from HIGHEST to LOWEST?
✅ Answer: B
GWP (100-year): SF₆ (Sulphur hexafluoride) = 23,900 | N₂O = 265 | CH₄ = 28 | CO₂ = 1 (reference). SF₆ used in electrical transformers is the most potent known GHG, though CO₂ has the largest total warming effect due to its sheer abundance in the atmosphere.
