Topic 1

Human Population Growth & Impacts

📈 The Population Explosion

The human population has grown exponentially over the last two centuries, primarily due to advances in medicine, sanitation, food security, and agriculture lowering death rates while birth rates remained relatively high.

  • Resource Depletion: High population density leads to rapid exploitation of water, minerals, fossil fuels, and arable land.
  • Environmental Degradation: Deforestation for urban sprawl, habitat fragmentation, and biodiversity loss (the Anthropocene epoch).
  • Pollution: Massive increases in municipal waste, industrial effluents, and greenhouse gases leading to climate change.
The IPAT Equation: Proposed by Ehrlich and Holdren:
Impact (I) = Population (P) × Affluence (A) × Technology (T)
This describes how environmental impact (I) is a function of the size of the population (P), consumption per capita or wealth (A), and the environmental impact of the technology used to produce that wealth (T).
Topic 2

Environmental Ethics & Culture

🧘🏽‍♂️ Philosophical Worldviews

Environmental ethics is the discipline that studies the moral relationship of human beings to, and also the value and moral status of, the environment and its non-human contents.

🚶‍♂️ Anthropocentrism

A human-centered view. Nature is seen merely as a resource or instrument to satisfy human needs and desires. Non-human entities have no intrinsic value.

🌿 Biocentrism

A life-centered view. All living organisms (plants and animals) have inherent worth and a right to exist, independent of their utility to humans.

🌎 Ecocentrism & Deep Ecology

Ecosystem-centered view. Intrinsic value is placed on whole ecological systems (including abiotic elements like soil, rivers, rocks). Coined by philosopher Arne Naess, Deep Ecology demands a fundamental shift in how humans interact with nature, advocating for lifestyle changes that match carrying capacity.

The great ecological dilemma — Industrial expansion vs. planetary boundary limits 🌎

Indian Culture and Traditional Conservation

Indian cultural practices are deeply tied to nature conservation:
• Peepal (Ficus religiosa) & Banyan trees are worshipped, preventing them from being cut.
• **Ahimsa** (non-violence) in Buddhism and Jainism protects animal life.
• **Sacred Groves:** Forest patches dedicated to local deities where all hunting and cutting are strictly prohibited, preserving rare species.

CNG Transition Case Study (Delhi): Facing severe air pollution, the Supreme Court of India in 1998 directed all public transport buses, autos, and taxis in Delhi to switch to **CNG (Compressed Natural Gas)**. It became one of the most successful judicial and public interventions, drastically reducing particulate matter (PM10) and carbon emissions.

Resettlement and Rehabilitation (R&R)

Developmental projects like dams, highways, and mines lead to large-scale displacement of local populations, primarily tribal communities (Project Affected Persons - PAPs). R&R is the policy of relocating these populations, providing them alternative livelihoods, housing, and compensation, although historically poor execution has led to major social conflicts.

Topic 3

Environmental Laws & Policies

⚖️ Constitutional Provisions in India

  • Article 48A (DPSP): Directs the State to protect and improve the environment and safeguard the forests and wildlife of the country.
  • Article 51A(g) (Fundamental Duties): It is the duty of every citizen of India to protect and improve the natural environment including forests, lakes, rivers, and wildlife.
  • Article 21 (Right to Life): The Supreme Court has interpreted the Right to Life to include the right to a clean, pollution-free, and healthy environment.

📜 Key Indian Environmental Acts

Summary of major environmental acts in India 📜

  • Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972: Restricts hunting of wild animals, establishes National Parks and Sanctuaries, and classifies protected species into Schedules.
  • Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974: Established the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards (SPCB) to monitor and maintain water quality.
  • Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980: Restricts the diversion of forest lands for non-forest purposes without central government permission.
  • Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981: Targets air quality control; amended in 1987 to include **Noise** under the definition of air pollutants.
  • Environment (Protection) Act, 1986: An umbrella legislation enacted in the wake of the 1984 Bhopal Gas Tragedy. It gives the central government broad power to set standards, coordinate state boards, and mandate Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA).

🤝 International Agreements & Protocols

  • Montreal Protocol (1987): An internationally binding treaty designed to protect the ozone layer by phasing out the production of Ozone Depleting Substances (ODS) like chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs). Highly successful.
  • Kyoto Protocol (1997): Commits industrialized nations to reduce greenhouse gas emissions based on the principle of **CBDR (Common But Differentiated Responsibilities)**. Introduced Carbon Trading and the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM).
  • Paris Agreement (2015): A global treaty aimed at limiting global warming to well below 2°C, preferably **1.5°C**, compared to pre-industrial levels, through Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs).
  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD - 1992): Focuses on conservation, sustainable use, and fair benefit sharing of genetic resources (supplemented by Cartagena and Nagoya Protocols).
Topic 4

Environmental Movements in India

🌳 Historical Grassroots Movements

Movement Key Leader / Location Core objective & Method
Bishnoi Sacrifice (1730) Amrita Devi Bishnoi / Khejarli, Rajasthan 363 Bishnois sacrificed their lives hugging Khejri trees to prevent them from being cut by the Jodhpur King's soldiers. Led to royal decree protecting trees and wildlife.
Chipko Movement (1973) Sunderlal Bahuguna, Gaura Devi / Garhwal, Uttarakhand Villagers, mainly women, hugged trees to stop commercial logging in the Himalayas. Slogan: "Ecology is permanent economy." Led to a 15-year ban on green felling.
Appiko Movement (1983) Pandurang Hegde / Western Ghats, Karnataka Inspired by Chipko ('Appiko' means to hug in Kannada). Aimed to protect tropical forests of Western Ghats from commercial felling.
Silent Valley Movement (1973) Kerala Sastra Sahitya Parishad / Kerala Protested a hydroelectric dam on the Kunthipuzha river that threatened the unique Silent Valley evergreen rainforests. Dam cancelled; declared a National Park.
Narmada Bachao Andolan (1985) Medha Patkar, Baba Amte / Gujarat & MP Mobilized tribals and farmers against displacement caused by building the Sardar Sarovar Dam on the Narmada River, demanding proper Rehabilitation & Resettlement.
Jungle Bachao Andolan (1982) Tribals of Singhbhum / Bihar (now Jharkhand) Protested the government's decision to replace natural, biodiverse Sal forests with commercial Teak plantations (known as 'Greed Game').
Tehri Dam Conflict Sunderlal Bahuguna / Uttarakhand Opposed building the giant Tehri Dam on the Bhagirathi River due to seismic threats (located in Zone V) and massive displacement of historical town.

Painting depicting Amrita Devi Bishnoi and her daughters hugging the Khejri trees in 1730 🌳
