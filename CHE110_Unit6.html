Unit 6 · Overview

🌍 Introduction

Unit 6 explores the complex relationship between human communities and the environment. As the human population grows, it exerts increasing pressure on natural systems — affecting air, water, land, biodiversity, and climate.

This unit covers:

  • Human population growth and its environmental & social impacts
  • Environmental ethics — our moral duties toward nature
  • Public awareness — spreading environmental knowledge
  • Cultural and tribal practices that support conservation
  • Key environmental laws enacted in India
  • Famous environmental movements that shaped India's conservation history
💡 Core Idea: Humans are both the cause of environmental degradation and the key to solving it — through ethics, awareness, law, and community action.
Topic 1

👥 Human Population Growth

Human population growth refers to the increase in the total number of people on Earth over time. It occurs mainly when birth rates exceed death rates.

📊 World population crossed 8 billion in 2022 and is projected to reach ~10 billion by 2058 (UN Population Division).

⚡ Causes of Population Growth

  • ✅ Advances in medicine: Cures for many life-threatening diseases have reduced death rates
  • ✅ High birth rates: Many communities still have high fertility rates
  • ✅ Son preference: Cultural pressure to have male children leads to larger families
  • ✅ High illiteracy: Lack of education reduces awareness of family planning
  • ✅ Poverty: Poor families may rely on more children for economic support
  • ✅ Lack of awareness: Limited access to family planning information
  • ✅ Early marriages: Child marriages lead to more reproductive years

💥 Effects of Overpopulation

  • Excessive exploitation of natural resources
  • Widespread unemployment
  • Inadequate health and sanitation facilities
  • Increased environmental pollution
  • Rising demand for food and water
  • Increased waste generation
  • Shortage of schools and medical facilities
  • Rise in crime rates
Topic 2

🌱 Impacts on Environment, Health & Welfare

🌏 Impact on Environment

  • Deforestation & Habitat Loss: Increased demand for land drives forest clearing
  • Resource Depletion: More food, water, and energy needed → heavy pressure on natural resources
  • Urban Sprawl: City expansion causes biodiversity loss and ecosystem fragmentation
  • Pollution: More waste = more contamination of air, water, and soil
  • Climate Change: Higher greenhouse gas emissions from growing industrial & domestic activity

🏥 Impact on Human Health & Welfare

  • Healthcare strain: Overpopulation reduces quality and accessibility of medical care
  • Disease spread: Overcrowding increases infectious diseases and worsens sanitation
  • Malnutrition: Scarcity of food and water leads to hunger and deficiencies
  • Poverty & Unemployment: High density reduces job availability and increases poverty
  • Slums: Housing pressure creates unsafe living conditions
  • Declining welfare: Resources and services can't keep pace with growth
🌿 Solutions: Education, family planning programs, women empowerment, sustainable development policies, and urbanization management can help slow population growth.
Topic 3

⚖️ Environmental Ethics

Environmental ethics is the branch of philosophy that studies the moral principles guiding human behavior toward nature and the environment.

💭 "Environmental ethics asks: Do we have moral duties to nature, to animals, and to future generations — not just to other humans today?"

🌿 Core Principles

  • Respect for all living beings and natural ecosystems
  • Sustainable use of resources to protect the planet
  • Minimizing pollution and reducing human environmental impact
  • Supporting conservation and preservation of biodiversity
  • Ensuring a healthy environment for present and future generations

📌 Key Ethical Principles

PrincipleMeaning
Environmental EquityAll living beings have an equal right to exist in a healthy environment
Priority PrincipleWhen human rights and environmental rights conflict, balance must be carefully sought
Sustainability PrincipleUse resources in a way that does not compromise future generations
Precautionary PrincipleWhen in doubt, err on the side of protecting the environment

✅ Practical Applications

  • Respect the power of the environment and apply it for human benefit responsibly
  • Prioritize health, safety, and environmental protection in all decisions
  • Be sensitive to ethical and social issues regarding the environment
  • Avoid activities that cause irreversible damage to ecosystems
📚 Types of Environmental Ethics:
Anthropocentrism – Human-centered; nature exists for human benefit
Biocentrism – All living beings have inherent value
Ecocentrism – Entire ecosystems (not just individuals) have value and rights

Public awareness refers to the degree to which people understand important environmental issues and their responsibilities toward solving them.

🔑 Why It Matters

  • Encourages responsible and eco-friendly behavior
  • Supports government and NGO efforts for environmental protection
  • Motivates collective action and policy change

Topic 5

🏺 Cultural Practices for Environmental Conservation

Many indigenous and traditional cultures around the world have developed practices that naturally protect and conserve the environment — often long before modern conservation science existed.

🌾 Traditional Practices

  • Crop rotation & organic manuring: Traditional farming methods that maintain soil health
  • Rainwater harvesting: Indigenous water conservation techniques
  • Protecting sacred animals & plants: Religious/spiritual beliefs protect certain species
  • Ritual restrictions on hunting & fishing: Protect wildlife during breeding seasons
  • Community-managed grazing: Prevents overgrazing and protects grasslands

Topic 6

🏕️ Tribal Populations & Environmental Conservation

Tribal (indigenous) communities are among the original stewards of Earth's natural environments. They have traditionally lived in harmony with nature for thousands of years.

🔍 Who Are Tribal Communities?

  • Live traditionally in forests, hilly areas, or remote regions
  • Have distinct cultures, languages, and customs
  • Follow unique social, economic, and religious practices
  • Depend closely on nature for their livelihood
  • Protected by special constitutional rights and policies
📝 Common Tribal Communities in India:
Gonds, Bhils, Santhals, Todas, Nagas, Mizos, Bhutias, Oraon, Khasi, Bodo, Jarawa, etc.

🌿 Role in Environmental Conservation

  • Forest Protection: Through traditional practices, sacred groves, and community rules
  • Sustainable Resource Use: Take only what is needed for livelihood
  • Biodiversity Conservation: Traditional ecological knowledge helps conserve plant and animal diversity
  • Wildlife Protection: Tribal taboos and rituals restrict hunting
  • Afforestation: Active participation in forest management and fire prevention
  • Low-impact Living: Lifestyle maintains ecological balance
✨ Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) is the accumulated wisdom of indigenous communities about local ecosystems — increasingly recognized by modern conservation science as invaluable.
Topic 7

⚖️ Environmental Laws in India

India has enacted a comprehensive set of environmental laws to regulate pollution, protect biodiversity, conserve forests, and ensure environmental justice.

🏛️ The Environment (Protection) Act, 1986

An umbrella legislation empowering the central government to protect and improve the environment, regulate pollution, and issue directives to industries.

💨 The Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981

Controls air pollution by regulating industrial emissions. Established Central and State Pollution Control Boards (CPCB & SPCB).

💧 The Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974

Ensures prevention and control of water pollution. Maintains water quality through regulatory bodies. India's first major environmental law.

🦁 The Wildlife Protection Act, 1972

Protects wildlife, national parks, sanctuaries, and endangered species. Prohibits hunting of scheduled animals.

🌲 The Forest Conservation Act, 1980

Restricts the use of forest land for non-forest purposes. Requires central government approval to divert forest land.

🧬 The Biological Diversity Act, 2002

Conserves biological resources and regulates access to genetic resources and traditional knowledge. Established National Biodiversity Authority (NBA).

☢️ The Hazardous Waste Management Rules (1989 & updated)

Regulates handling, storage, transportation, and disposal of hazardous and toxic waste.

🏛️ The National Green Tribunal (NGT) Act, 2010

Establishes a special court (tribunal) to address environmental disputes and ensure speedy justice. India is among few nations with such a dedicated environmental court.

🛡️ The Public Liability Insurance Act, 1991

Provides immediate relief to people affected by accidents involving hazardous substances. Companies must carry insurance for such incidents.

🌊 The Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) Notification, 2011

Regulates activities along coastal areas to protect marine ecosystems and prevent unplanned development near coastlines.

Topic 8

✊ Environmental Movements in India

India has a rich history of grassroots environmental activism. These movements show how ordinary citizens — farmers, women, tribal communities — have fought to protect their environment.

1730 AD · Rajasthan

🌳 Bishnois of Rajasthan

The Bishnoi community, guided by Guru Jambheshwar's 29 principles, have long protected nature. In the 1730 Khejarli Massacre, 363 Bishnois — led by Amrita Devi — sacrificed their lives to protect khejri trees from being felled for a king's palace. They protect blackbucks and chinkaras as sacred animals. This event inspired India's conservation movements.

1973 · Uttarakhand

🤗 Chipko Movement

Started in Chamoli district in 1973. Local villagers — especially women of Reni village led by Gaura Devi — hugged trees to prevent commercial logging. Key leaders included Chandi Prasad Bhatt and Sunderlal Bahuguna. "Chipko" means "to cling." It succeeded in halting logging and led to a 15-year ban on tree felling in the Himalayas.

1983 · Karnataka

🌿 Appiko Movement

Inspired by Chipko, this movement in Karnataka's Western Ghats adopted the same tree-hugging method ("Appiko" = embrace in Kannada). Led by Pandurang Hegde with support from villagers, women, and children. Its three themes: Regeneration, Conservation, and Sustainable Use. Successfully halted tree felling in several districts.

1973–1983 · Kerala

🏔️ Silent Valley Movement

Fought to protect the tropical rainforest of Silent Valley in Kerala's Palakkad district. The Kerala State Electricity Board proposed a hydroelectric dam on the Kunthipuzha River. The forest was home to the endangered lion-tailed macaque. Activists like Dr. Salim Ali and Sunderlal Bahuguna opposed it. Indira Gandhi declared protection in 1981; the project was cancelled in November 1983. Silent Valley became a National Park in 1985 (inaugurated by Rajiv Gandhi).

1982 · Jharkhand/Odisha

🪵 Jungle Bachao Andolan

Tribal communities in Jharkhand and Odisha protested the felling of sal forests and their replacement with commercial plantations like teak. The movement emphasized forest rights, conservation, and sustainable use. It strengthened tribal participation in environmental activism and asserted communities' right over local forest resources.

1985 · Madhya Pradesh/Gujarat

💧 Narmada Bachao Andolan

Led by Medha Patkar, this movement opposed the Sardar Sarovar Dam and large dams on the Narmada River. The Narmada River Development Project planned over 3,000 dams, threatening to submerge 245 villages, displace 200,000+ people, and destroy ecologically sensitive areas — mainly affecting Adivasi, Dalit, and marginalized communities. NBA demanded proper rehabilitation and environmental assessment.

Late 1970s–2000s · Uttarakhand

🌊 Tehri Dam Conflict

Controversy over a large hydroelectric dam on the Bhagirathi River in Tehri, Uttarakhand. The dam was planned for electricity, irrigation, and water supply. Construction submerged the old Tehri town and displaced thousands. Environmental concerns included deforestation, landslides, and seismic risk. Sunderlal Bahuguna led protests in the 1980s. Despite activism, the dam was completed — a reminder that not all movements succeed.

Key Concepts

💡 Important Concepts at a Glance

Birth Rate

Number of live births per 1000 people per year. High birth rate = faster population growth.

Death Rate

Number of deaths per 1000 people per year. When birth > death, population grows.

Carrying Capacity

Maximum population an environment can sustainably support. Overpopulation exceeds this limit.

Demographic Transition

Shift from high birth+death rates to low birth+death rates as a country develops economically.

Biocentrism

Ethical view that all living beings have inherent value — not just humans.

Ecocentrism

Ethical view that entire ecosystems (rivers, forests) have rights and intrinsic value.

Sacred Groves

Forest patches protected by communities for religious/cultural reasons. India has thousands.

TEK

Traditional Ecological Knowledge — indigenous wisdom about local ecosystems, used for sustainable resource management.

NGT

National Green Tribunal — India's specialized court for fast resolution of environmental disputes (est. 2010).

CPCB / SPCB

Central/State Pollution Control Boards — established under Water Act 1974 and Air Act 1981 to monitor pollution.

Wrap Up

📖 Unit Summary

Unit 6 draws a powerful connection between human communities and their environment:

  • Population Growth drives environmental degradation through deforestation, pollution, and resource depletion
  • Environmental Ethics provides the moral foundation for sustainable living and responsible behavior
  • Public Awareness — through education and media — is essential for behavioral change
  • Cultural and Tribal Knowledge offers time-tested, sustainable approaches to living with nature
  • Environmental Laws in India provide a strong legal framework for pollution control and nature conservation
  • Environmental Movements demonstrate that collective community action can shape national policy

🌍 Core Message of Unit 6

Human communities are the biggest threat to the environment — but also its greatest hope. Through ethics, law, awareness, and grassroots action, people can protect and restore the planet.
